Computing and AI Ethics is a complete, open-access textbook on the philosophical, social, and ethical dimensions of information technology and artificial intelligence. It takes seriously the questions that matter most as computing reshapes every aspect of human life: Who controls information — and who is harmed when that control is abused? What do we owe each other in a world of algorithmic decision-making, pervasive surveillance, and automated labor? What would it mean for an artificial mind to have rights?

The book traces recurring ethical patterns across five centuries of information technology — from the printing press to generative AI — and applies frameworks from virtue ethics, rights theory, and consequentialism to contemporary cases. Each of the twelve chapters is available as an extended HTML reading, a downloadable PDF slide deck, and (for selected chapters) an interactive self-check quiz. No prior background in philosophy or computer science is required.

About This Textbook

HTML Chapter Readings Extended readings for all twelve chapters, featuring key thinker profiles, argument boxes, case studies, definition boxes, and thought questions. Designed to develop the ideas from the lecture slides in greater depth.
Interactive Quizzes Auto-graded quizzes with detailed explanations for each answer. Three question types: multiple choice, select-all-that-apply, and matching. Your progress is saved in your browser so you can return where you left off or retry missed questions. Available for all twelve chapters.
Lecture Slides (PDF) Beamer slides in PDF format, compiled from LaTeX source. Available for all twelve chapters. LaTeX source is in the GitHub repository.
