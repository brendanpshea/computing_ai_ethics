### Section I: The Case for Existential Risk (~6-8 slides)
**Key thinkers**: Bostrom, Yudkowsky, Ord, Russell, Tegmark

Content:
- **What is existential risk?** Definition from Bostrom (2002): risks that threaten permanent curtailment of humanity's potential
- **The intelligence explosion hypothesis**: Bostrom's superintelligence argument — an AI surpassing human intelligence could recursively self-improve
- **The orthogonality thesis**: Intelligence and goals are independent; a superintelligent system could pursue any objective
- **The instrumental convergence thesis**: Almost any goal leads to self-preservation, resource acquisition, etc.
- **Yudkowsky's warning**: The alignment problem as fundamentally unsolved; "the AI does not love you, nor does it hate you"
- **Ord's risk estimates**: *The Precipice* places existential risk from unaligned AI at ~1 in 10 this century
- **The CAIS Statement (2023)**: Hundreds of researchers signing "Mitigating the risk of extinction from AI should be a global priority"
- **Stuart Russell's argument**: The "King Midas problem" — giving AI the wrong objective function

**Box types to use**: `argumentbox` for the formal arguments, `quotebox` for key quotes from Bostrom/Yudkowsky/Ord, `conceptbox` for definitions of existential risk, orthogonality thesis, etc.

**Possible image**: Doomsday Clock (Bulletin of Atomic Scientists) — public domain photo from Wikimedia

### Section II: The Critics Strike Back (~6-8 slides)
**Key thinkers**: Vallor, Narayanan, Gebru, Torres, Bender

Content:
- **Vallor's critique**: *The AI Mirror* (2024) — we project our fears and fantasies onto AI. The real danger isn't sentient machines but our willingness to cede moral agency to systems that don't understand us
- **Narayanan & Kapoor**: *AI Snake Oil* (2024) — much of the AI hype (including doomsday hype) rests on exaggerated capabilities. Predictive AI is often pseudoscience
- **The "Stochastic Parrots" argument**: Bender & Gebru (2021) — LLMs don't "understand" anything; existential risk framing distracts from real harms (bias, environmental cost, labor exploitation)
- **Gebru & Torres on TESCREAL**: The ideological roots of longtermism — connections to transhumanism, effective altruism, and (they argue) eugenic thinking
- **Torres's "Against Longtermism"**: Why prioritizing hypothetical future billions over present suffering is morally questionable
- **The distraction argument**: Focusing on speculative superintelligence diverts resources and attention from algorithmic discrimination, surveillance, and platform harms happening *now*

**Box types**: `objectionbox` for the critiques, `quotebox` for Vallor/Gebru/Torres quotes, `discussionbox` for student debate prompts

### Section III: Autonomous Weapons and AI in Warfare (~5-7 slides)
**Key thinkers**: Scharre, Human Rights Watch

Content:
- **What are lethal autonomous weapons systems (LAWS)?** Definition and spectrum from human-in-the-loop to fully autonomous
- **Current state of military AI**: Drones, targeting systems, and the battlefield of the near future
- **The "Campaign to Stop Killer Robots"**: HRW's *Losing Humanity* report (2012) and the push for a preemptive ban
- **Scharre's analysis**: *Army of None* — the complex ethics of delegating kill decisions to machines
- **The accountability gap**: If an autonomous weapon kills a civilian, who is responsible? The programmer? The commander? The machine?
- **Case study**: A real-world case (e.g., drone warfare in recent conflicts, the Kargu-2 in Libya)
- **Arguments for and against**: Proponents say autonomous weapons could reduce civilian casualties through precision; critics say they lower the threshold for war

**Box types**: `casestudybox` for military case studies, `argumentbox` for pro/con arguments, `conceptbox` for LAWS definition

### Section IV: AI and Biological Threats (~4-5 slides)
**Key thinker**: Sandbrink

Content:
- **The dual-use dilemma**: AI accelerates biological research (AlphaFold, drug discovery) but could also help create bioweapons
- **LLMs vs. biological design tools**: Sandbrink's distinction — LLMs provide accessible information, while specialized protein/DNA design tools pose more novel risks
- **Case study**: The 2022 experiment where researchers asked a drug-discovery AI to generate toxic compounds — it produced VX nerve agent analogs in hours
- **How worried should we be?** The "information hazard" concept — some knowledge is dangerous to spread
- **Governance responses**: Screening for dangerous sequences, responsible AI in biology, dual-use research oversight

**Box types**: `casestudybox`, `conceptbox` for dual-use and information hazard

**Image**: Laboratory/biosafety (public domain)

### Section V: Longtermism and the Precautionary Principle (~5-6 slides)
**Key thinkers**: MacAskill, Sunstein, Torres

Content:
- **What is longtermism?** MacAskill's *What We Owe the Future* — the idea that positively influencing the long-term future is a key moral priority of our time
- **The expected value argument**: Even small probabilities of extinction justify enormous investment in prevention (Pascal's Mugging?)
- **The precautionary principle**: When an activity poses potential catastrophic harm, the burden of proof falls on those who want to proceed
- **Sunstein's critique of the precautionary principle**: *Laws of Fear* — the principle is incoherent because every action (and inaction) carries risks. Regulation should be based on cost-benefit analysis
- **Competing moral frameworks**: Longtermism (utilitarian, speculative) vs. present-focused justice (addressing algorithmic bias, labor exploitation, environmental harm NOW)
- **TikZ diagram**: A visual comparing longtermist vs. present-focused approaches to AI risk

**Box types**: `conceptbox` for longtermism and precautionary principle, `probox` and `objectionbox` for competing arguments, `discussionbox`

### Section VI: Conclusion — Living with Uncertainty (~3-4 slides)

Content:
- **Key thinkers summary table**: Comparing Bostrom, Yudkowsky, Ord, Russell, Tegmark, Vallor, Narayanan, Gebru, Torres, Scharre, MacAskill (similar to the thinker tables in other chapters)
- **Synthesis**: We don't need to choose between existential risk and present harms — both deserve attention. But *how we frame* the problem shapes *who gets resources* and *whose voice counts*
- **Virtue ethics perspective**: Vallor's technomoral virtues — humility, justice, courage, care — as a way to navigate genuine uncertainty without either panic or complacency
- **Final discussion question**: "If you had $1 billion to spend on reducing AI-related risks, how would you allocate it between: (a) AI alignment research, (b) autonomous weapons regulation, (c) addressing present-day algorithmic bias, and (d) AI biosecurity? Justify your allocation."
