What Is Information Technology?

In the summer of 399 BCE, the philosopher Socrates stood before a jury of five hundred Athenian citizens and was condemned to death. His alleged crime was corrupting the youth of Athens. His real offense, many historians believe, was something more specific: he questioned everything, including the authority of those who claimed to know best. He died having written nothing — not a single word. Everything we know about Socrates comes through other people's accounts, above all through the dialogues written by his most famous student, Plato. There is a deep irony here that Socrates himself would have appreciated. He distrusted writing. He thought it was a poor substitute for real conversation. And yet he lives in history only because someone wrote about him.

This chapter begins with Socrates because his skepticism toward writing captures something important about every major information technology revolution: they always look different from the inside than they do from the outside. The people inventing and adopting a new technology tend to see its possibilities. The critics tend to see the risks. History usually proves both groups partially right — and the surprises tend to be larger than anyone predicted.

Before diving into specific revolutions, we need two definitions. The first is the subject of this entire course.

Information Technology (IT)

Any technology used to create, store, transmit, or process information. By this definition, information technology is ancient — writing, the printing press, the telegraph, and the smartphone are all IT, differing in speed and scale but not in kind.

This broad definition matters because it prevents us from treating the digital revolution as something completely unprecedented. Every generation has faced the question of what new communication technologies mean for society, for truth, and for power. The second definition frames how we will evaluate the answers different thinkers have proposed.

Normative Ethics

The branch of philosophy concerned with how people ought to act — with identifying the principles that distinguish right from wrong. Unlike descriptive ethics (what people actually believe) or metaethics (whether moral facts exist at all), normative ethics aims to guide conduct.

This course is primarily concerned with normative ethics applied to technology: not just describing what societies have done with their information tools, but evaluating whether they did it well. The five revolutions we examine are ordered chronologically, and each one will give us new concepts and case studies to bring to that evaluative project.

Revolution 1: Writing

Around 3200 BCE, in the city-states of ancient Mesopotamia — the region we now call Iraq — scribes began pressing reed styluses into wet clay tablets to produce what we recognize as the world's first writing system: cuneiform. Its early uses were mundane. Archaeologists have found ancient records of grain allocations, cattle inventories, and commercial debts. Writing began as a technology for keeping score. Yet within a few centuries it had transformed into something far more consequential: a tool for preserving law, transmitting religion, accumulating science, and producing literature. The Epic of Gilgamesh, composed around 2100 BCE and surviving on clay tablets, is one of the earliest pieces of literature in human history — a meditation on mortality, friendship, and the search for meaning that would not feel out of place in a contemporary bookstore.

The invention of writing externalizes memory. Before writing, knowledge lived inside people: in the minds of elders, in the performances of bards, in the accumulated skills of craftspeople. When those people died, their knowledge died with them unless it had been passed on through direct instruction. Writing broke this cycle. Suddenly, a legal code could outlast its author by millennia. A mathematical proof could be checked and built upon by people who never met its originator. A religious text could reach believers scattered across continents.

Not everyone celebrated this transformation. In ancient Athens, a philosopher named Socrates looked at writing and saw something troubling: a technology that would give people the appearance of wisdom without the reality.

Key Thinker

Plato (c. 428–348 BCE)

Plato was born into an aristocratic Athenian family and became the most influential student of Socrates. After Socrates' execution in 399 BCE, Plato traveled widely before returning to Athens to found the Academy — one of the first institutions of higher learning in Western history, which operated for nearly nine hundred years. His works, all written as dialogues, explore ethics, politics, metaphysics, and epistemology.

Here lies the great irony: Plato preserved Socrates' oral philosophy in writing. In the Phaedrus, Plato records Socrates making an argument against writing itself. We only know this argument because someone wrote it down. Socrates famously wrote nothing. He taught through conversation, believing that genuine understanding could only be reached through live dialogue, not by reading fixed texts. Plato spent his life honoring his teacher's ideas by doing exactly what his teacher distrusted.

Plato's other major works include the Republic — a vision of an ideal state ruled by philosopher-kings — and the Apology, his account of Socrates' trial and death. His theory of Forms, which holds that abstract concepts like Justice and Beauty are more real than physical objects, shaped Western philosophy for two thousand years.

The Myth of Theuth and Thamus

In the Phaedrus, Plato has Socrates tell a story — a myth — about the invention of writing. The Egyptian god Theuth has invented writing and presents it to Thamus, the king of Egypt, for his royal approval. Theuth is enthusiastic: writing, he claims, will make the Egyptians wiser and improve their memory. Thamus disagrees, sharply. He argues that Theuth has invented not a memory aid but a reminder system — and a dangerous one at that.

For this invention will produce forgetfulness in the minds of those who learn to use it, because they will not practice their memory. Their trust in writing, produced by external characters which are no part of themselves, will discourage the use of their own memory within them. You have invented an elixir not of memory, but of reminding; and you offer your pupils the appearance of wisdom, not true wisdom, for they will read many things without instruction and will therefore seem to know many things, when they are for the most part ignorant and hard to get along with, since they are not wise, but only appear wise.

This passage repays careful attention. Thamus draws a precise distinction between memory — actively held, internalized knowledge — and reminding — the passive retrieval of externally stored information. His worry is not that the information in a written text is false. His worry is that readers will mistake the ability to look something up for the ability to actually understand it. They will "seem to know many things" while actually knowing very little. Does this concern sound familiar? It should. The same objection has been raised about Google, about Wikipedia, about calculators, and about AI chatbots. Every generation has faced a version of Thamus's worry: does this new tool help us think, or does it think for us?

What's the Argument?

Socrates's Argument Against Writing

  1. Genuine knowledge requires active engagement: the ability to respond to questions, to adapt an explanation to a particular audience, and to examine one's own reasoning.
  2. A written text cannot respond to questions or adapt to its reader — it always says the same thing to everyone.
  3. Relying on written texts therefore produces a false sense of understanding: readers feel informed without having genuinely grasped the subject.
∴ Writing produces the appearance of wisdom, not genuine wisdom, and is therefore inferior to living dialogue for developing real understanding.

Is this argument convincing? There is something undeniably right about it. Students who can recite a definition from their notes without being able to apply or explain it have experienced exactly what Socrates describes. And yet the argument seems too strong. The accumulated knowledge of human civilization — mathematics, science, literature, history, law — would be impossible without writing. Socrates himself benefited from reading: he was deeply familiar with Homer, with earlier philosophers, with Athenian drama. The argument identifies a real risk — confusing external records with internal understanding — without proving that the risk outweighs the gains.

Case Study

Hammurabi's Code (c. 1754 BCE)

Among the most striking early uses of writing is the Code of Hammurabi, a Babylonian legal code inscribed on an eight-foot basalt stele around 1754 BCE. Commissioned by King Hammurabi of Babylon, the code contains 282 laws governing everything from wages and property disputes to marriage, adoption, and criminal punishment. Its famous principle — "an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth" — is often quoted out of context; in fact, the code represented a significant constraint on arbitrary royal power, since the king's judges were now bound by written rules rather than personal whim.

Yet the same technology served opposite ends. The stele was publicly displayed — anyone (who could read) could check whether a judge's ruling conformed to written law. But the administrative apparatus that writing enabled also empowered an imperial bureaucracy. Tax records, tribute lists, and grain accounting allowed Hammurabi to manage an empire of hundreds of thousands of people with a precision impossible in purely oral societies. Writing was simultaneously a check on arbitrary power and a tool for consolidating it.

This paradox is characteristic of every IT revolution: the same technology that enables legal accountability also enables administrative surveillance and control.

Thought Questions

  1. Socrates worried that writing would make people seem wise without being wise. Do you think the internet has made this problem better or worse? What evidence would you cite?
  2. What would be lost if humanity lost all written records tomorrow? What, if anything, would be gained?
  3. Is Socrates's argument against writing a good argument? Try to identify its strongest and weakest premise.
  4. Hammurabi's Code was both a protection against arbitrary power and a tool of imperial control. Can you think of a modern technology with the same double-edged quality?

Revolution 2: The Printing Press

On October 31, 1517, a thirty-three-year-old monk and theology professor named Martin Luther nailed a list of ninety-five theses — arguments he wanted to debate — to the door of a church in Wittenberg, Germany. This was a standard academic gesture, common in universities of the time. What was not standard was what happened next. Within two weeks, printed copies of Luther's theses had spread across Germany. Within two months, they had reached most of Europe. Within a few years, his German translation of the Bible had become the best-selling book on the continent. None of this would have been possible without the technology that a German goldsmith named Johannes Gutenberg had developed about seventy years earlier: movable type printing.

Before Gutenberg, books were handwritten, one at a time, by scribes — usually monks in monasteries. A single copy of the Bible might take a skilled scribe years to produce and cost as much as a house. In 1450, there were approximately thirty thousand books in all of Europe. By 1500, fifty years after Gutenberg's press began operating, there were approximately ten million. The cost of a book had fallen by ninety percent. Literacy began to spread beyond the clergy and aristocracy. The monopoly that the Catholic Church had long maintained over the production and interpretation of written texts was shattered.

✝

Key Thinker

Martin Luther (1483–1546)

Martin Luther was a German Augustinian friar, theologian, and professor whose protests against the Catholic Church's practice of selling "indulgences" — payments that supposedly reduced time in purgatory — launched the Protestant Reformation. His 95 Theses questioned papal authority over salvation, and his subsequent writings developed a theology centered on faith alone (sola fide) and Scripture alone (sola scriptura).

Luther was the first major thinker to recognize the printing press as a world-historical tool. He wrote and published with extraordinary speed and productivity; his complete works fill over a hundred volumes. He saw the press as providential: a divine instrument for spreading the Gospel in its pure form, without institutional mediation. His German translation of the Bible standardized the German language and made Scripture accessible to ordinary people who could not read Latin.

Luther's legacy is complicated. The same convictions that led him to challenge tyrannical authority also led him to support the brutal suppression of the Peasants' Revolt (1525) and to write virulently anti-Semitic texts late in his life — texts that were later cited by Nazi propagandists. He is a powerful example of how the same technology can serve both liberation and oppression, depending on who is using it and why.

Luther and Erasmus: Two Visions of the Press

Luther's great contemporary, the Dutch humanist scholar Desiderius Erasmus, was also a prolific author who benefited enormously from the printing press. Erasmus's satirical work The Praise of Folly was a bestseller. He corresponded with scholars across Europe, and his critical edition of the Greek New Testament — produced using printed books from multiple libraries — was a monument of Renaissance scholarship. Yet Erasmus viewed Luther's use of the press with deep unease.

For Erasmus, the problem was not the press itself but the use to which Luther put it: flooding the reading public with inflammatory, doctrinally complex arguments that, in Erasmus's view, most readers were not equipped to evaluate. Where Luther saw a marketplace of ideas in which truth would triumph, Erasmus saw a crowded and dangerous bazaar where the loudest voice, not the wisest, would win. Reform, he believed, should come from careful scholars working within the Church, not from mass popular movements whipped up by pamphlets. History suggests both men were partially right.

What's the Argument?

The Erasmian Argument for Gatekeeping

  1. Rapid, uncontrolled dissemination of complex ideas leads to widespread misunderstanding and social conflict.
  2. Most readers lack the training to evaluate complex theological, scientific, or philosophical arguments without expert guidance.
  3. Printing enables such ideas to reach mass audiences without any expert mediation.
∴ Some degree of gatekeeping — scholarly, editorial, or institutional — is necessary to prevent the printing press from doing more harm than good.

Supporting argument

The evidence supports Erasmus's concern. The fifty years following Luther's 95 Theses saw not only the spread of literacy and biblical knowledge but also the witch-hunting manual Malleus Maleficarum becoming a bestseller, the religious wars that devastated central Europe, and inflammatory anti-Semitic pamphlets spreading with unprecedented speed. The press did not just spread truth — it amplified every dangerous idea with equal indifference.

Objection

But who gets to be the gatekeeper? In Luther's day, the gatekeepers were the Catholic Church and secular princes who banned and burned books on the basis of their own interests. Erasmus himself was censured by Church authorities. A gatekeeping system tends to protect the powerful, not the truth. Erasmus's argument, however reasonable it sounds in the abstract, risks being a defense of the status quo against any challenge from below.

The Luther-Erasmus debate is the prototype for an argument that recurs across every subsequent information revolution: between those who want to maximize the free flow of information and those who argue that some filtering is necessary to prevent harm. We will encounter it again when we discuss internet content moderation in Chapter 3 and AI-generated misinformation in Chapter 7.

Case Study

The 95 Theses and the Speed of Print (1517)

Luther posted his 95 Theses on October 31, 1517. Within two weeks, friends had them printed in Leipzig, Nuremberg, and Basel. Within two months, they had circulated to virtually every corner of the German-speaking world and into France, England, and Italy. This was staggeringly fast by the standards of the time. A manuscript letter might take weeks to travel the same distance. The Pope, receiving reports from Rome about an obscure German monk's complaint, initially dismissed the matter as a "monks' quarrel." By the time the Church recognized the seriousness of the situation, Luther's ideas had already taken root across an entire continent. The press had changed the speed of intellectual history.

What this case illustrates is a property of information technology that recurs throughout history: new technologies change not just the reach of ideas but their velocity. An idea that spreads slowly gives institutions time to respond, refute, and adapt. An idea that spreads in days does not. This acceleration creates both opportunity (marginalized voices can reach audiences quickly) and danger (misinformation can spread faster than correction). In the sixteenth century, this dynamic helped shatter the thousand-year institutional unity of Western Christianity. In the twenty-first, it shapes the spread of everything from political disinformation to scientific discoveries.

Thought Questions

  1. Erasmus worried that the press would spread ideas too quickly for ordinary readers to evaluate them. Does social media have the same problem? Is the problem worse or better now?
  2. Luther said the printing press was "the last and greatest gift" for spreading true religion. Can a technology be a gift for spreading truth if it is equally useful for spreading falsehood?
  3. Who are the "gatekeepers" of information in 2024? What legitimacy do they have — and who watches the watchmen?

Revolution 3: Distance Communication

On May 24, 1844, Samuel Morse sat in the chambers of the U.S. Supreme Court in Washington, D.C., and tapped out the first long-distance telegraph message in history, transmitting it forty miles to Baltimore. The message, chosen by a young woman named Annie Ellsworth from the Book of Numbers, read: "What hath God wrought!" It is hard to know whether the exclamation point reflects astonishment or gratitude, but either would be appropriate. The telegraph was, by the standards of its time, magic. Information that previously took days to travel by horseback arrived in seconds. Before the telegraph, the fastest communication in the world moved at the speed of a horse. After it, messages could circle the globe at the speed of electricity.

Nineteenth-century commentators responded to the telegraph with extravagant enthusiasm. Newspapers declared that it would unite all nations, abolish war, and usher in an era of universal peace and understanding. When the first transatlantic telegraph cable was completed in 1866, connecting North America and Europe permanently, it was celebrated as one of the greatest achievements in human history. The historian Tom Standage has called the telegraph "the Victorian Internet," and the comparison is apt in more ways than one: the same utopian claims made about the telegraph in the 1860s were made almost word for word about the internet in the 1990s.

Two of the most important thinkers of the nineteenth century, the British philosopher John Stuart Mill and the German philosopher Karl Marx, offered sharply contrasting analyses of what communications technology meant for society. Their disagreement goes to the heart of a debate that still divides us: Is technology a neutral tool that enables individual freedom, or is it always shaped by — and in service of — whoever holds economic and political power?

Key Thinker

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873)

John Stuart Mill was a British philosopher, economist, and Member of Parliament who became the most influential liberal thinker of the nineteenth century. He was educated with extraordinary intensity by his father, James Mill — learning Greek at age three and Latin at eight — in an experiment in radical rationalist education that he later described as both a blessing and a cause of serious psychological collapse in his twenties.

Mill's most enduring contribution to political philosophy is his 1859 book On Liberty, which argues for the maximum possible sphere of individual freedom constrained only by the "harm principle": you may do as you like until your actions harm others. In the domain of ideas, Mill was an absolute advocate for free speech, arguing that even false opinions must be allowed expression because the collision of ideas is how truth advances. He called this the "marketplace of ideas."

Mill also championed women's suffrage and worked throughout his career to extend the benefits of liberal society to those excluded from it. He saw communications technology as, on balance, a force for individual liberation and the spread of beneficial ideas.

Key Thinker

Karl Marx (1818–1883)

Karl Marx was a German philosopher, economist, and revolutionary who, with his collaborator Friedrich Engels, developed the theory of historical materialism and founded modern communist thought. Born into a middle-class Jewish family in Prussia, Marx was radicalized by his studies of Hegel and his observations of industrial capitalism's human costs. He was expelled from Germany, France, and Belgium before settling permanently in London, where he spent years researching in the British Museum while living in poverty.

Marx's central insight for our purposes is that technology is never politically neutral. It is always developed, owned, and deployed within a specific economic system, and it tends to serve the interests of those who control it. "The ruling ideas of each age," he wrote, "have ever been the ideas of its ruling class." Applied to communications technology: whoever owns the newspapers, the telegraph wires, or the social media platforms controls what ideas reach the public — and on what terms.

This does not mean Marx was always right. His predictions about the inevitable collapse of capitalism proved incorrect, and regimes claiming to act in his name produced some of the worst tyrannies in history. But his framework for thinking about technology and power remains essential and is applied by thinkers across the political spectrum today.

If all mankind minus one were of one opinion, and only one person were of the contrary opinion, mankind would be no more justified in silencing that one person than he, if he had the power, would be justified in silencing mankind.

Mill's argument rests on the claim that truth tends to emerge from open debate. If an opinion is right and is suppressed, we lose the truth. If it is wrong and is suppressed, we lose the opportunity to understand why it is wrong — and an unexamined belief is a fragile one. This is a powerful argument for freedom of expression. But it assumes that the "marketplace of ideas" operates fairly: that all voices have equal access and that the best arguments tend to win. Marx's analysis challenges precisely this assumption.

What's the Argument?

Marx's Argument: Technology Serves Its Owners

  1. In any society, those who control the means of production — including the means of communication — have decisive influence over public opinion.
  2. Under capitalism, the means of communication (printing houses, telegraph companies, later broadcast networks and social media platforms) are owned by the wealthy.
  3. Owners use their control to promote ideas that serve their interests and to suppress or marginalize ideas that threaten them.
∴ Mill's "free marketplace of ideas" is a fiction: the market is rigged in favor of those who own the infrastructure, and "free" communication in a capitalist society primarily amplifies the ideas of the ruling class.

You do not need to be a Marxist to find this argument troubling. Consider: in the United States today, most major media outlets are owned by a small number of large corporations. The handful of social media platforms through which most people receive their news are operated by some of the wealthiest companies in history. The algorithmic decisions these platforms make — which stories are shown, which voices are amplified, which content is suppressed — have enormous consequences for public discourse. Mill's dream of an open marketplace and Marx's warning about concentrated ownership are both relevant to this situation, and neither analysis alone is sufficient.

Thought Questions

  1. Mill argues that even false opinions should be allowed because debate is how we find truth. Does this argument work on social media, where algorithms determine which voices are amplified?
  2. Marx argues that whoever owns the means of communication controls the dominant ideas. Can you think of examples that support this claim? Counterexamples?
  3. Is there a way to have both Mill's open debate and a check on the power that Marx warns about? What would it look like in practice?

Revolution 4: Mass Media

The first three information revolutions — writing, printing, distance communication — shared a structural feature: they primarily enabled information to move between individuals or small groups. A letter goes from one person to another. A printed pamphlet reaches thousands, but each reader reads alone. The fourth revolution, the rise of radio and then television in the twentieth century, introduced something qualitatively new: broadcast — the simultaneous transmission of identical content to millions of passive recipients. A single radio broadcast could, for the first time in history, address an entire nation at once.

The implications of this shift were enormous. Radio and television created genuinely shared cultural experiences at national scale. They enabled rapid dissemination of public health information, political news, and educational content. But they also created a new kind of vulnerability: a small number of people controlling the broadcast infrastructure could shape the beliefs and emotions of a mass audience in ways that earlier media could not match. Two thinkers who witnessed the most catastrophic demonstration of this vulnerability — the rise of totalitarianism in Europe — provide our framework for this revolution.

✍

Key Thinker

George Orwell (1903–1950)

George Orwell — born Eric Arthur Blair in British India — was a novelist, essayist, and journalist who spent his career bearing witness to the political catastrophes of the twentieth century. He served as a colonial police officer in Burma, fought in the Spanish Civil War on the Republican side (where he was shot through the throat and narrowly survived), and observed at first hand the way both fascist and Stalinist propaganda worked to distort reality and silence dissent.

His two most famous works, Animal Farm (1945) and Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949), are written as fiction but function as extended philosophical arguments about the relationship between language, truth, and power. Nineteen Eighty-Four in particular contains some of the most trenchant analysis of propaganda ever written. Orwell's central concern was the deliberate corruption of language — the use of official terminology that means the opposite of what it seems to say ("War is Peace, Freedom is Slavery") — as a tool for eliminating political thought altogether.

Orwell died of tuberculosis in 1950 at the age of forty-six, shortly after finishing Nineteen Eighty-Four. His coinages — "doublethink," "memory hole," "Big Brother," "Newspeak," "thoughtcrime" — have entered the English language as permanent contributions to our vocabulary for describing political manipulation.

The Party told you to reject the evidence of your eyes and ears. It was their final, most essential command. His heart sank as he thought of the enormous power arrayed against him… And yet he was in the right! They were wrong and he was right. The obvious, the silly, and the true had got to be defended. Truisms are true, hold on to that!

The passage describes what Orwell saw as the ultimate goal of totalitarian propaganda: not merely to make people believe false things, but to destroy their capacity to distinguish true from false at all. Once that capacity is gone, the regime no longer needs to win arguments — it just needs to repeat assertions loudly enough and often enough. Mass broadcast media, in Orwell's analysis, was particularly dangerous because it could reach everyone simultaneously with the same message, eliminating the diversity of information sources that might otherwise give people the resources to resist.

Key Thinker

Hannah Arendt (1906–1975)

Hannah Arendt was a German-Jewish philosopher who fled Nazi Germany in 1933, spent eighteen years as a stateless refugee, and eventually became one of the most important political philosophers of the twentieth century. Her experience of persecution, statelessness, and exile gave her an outsider's perspective on the political institutions most Western thinkers took for granted.

In The Origins of Totalitarianism (1951), Arendt offered an analysis of how Nazi Germany and Stalinist Russia had been possible — not as aberrations, but as outcomes of specific historical and social conditions. Her analysis is distinctive in focusing not primarily on ideology but on loneliness: the breakdown of the social bonds and shared public world that give individuals a stable sense of reality. People who feel genuinely isolated, she argued, become susceptible to movements that offer belonging and a totalizing explanation of the world.

Arendt is also famous for her coverage of the 1961 trial of Adolf Eichmann, a Nazi war criminal responsible for organizing the transportation of Jews to death camps. Her concept of the "banality of evil" — her observation that Eichmann was not a monster but a bureaucrat who had stopped thinking — remains one of the most disturbing and important ideas in modern ethics.

What's the Argument?

Arendt's Argument: Mass Media and Social Atomization

  1. Industrial modernity uproots people from stable communities, traditional roles, and inherited social bonds, producing widespread social isolation — what Arendt calls "atomization."
  2. Atomized individuals, lacking a shared social world, become desperate for coherence, belonging, and a sense that their lives matter.
  3. Mass broadcast media can deliver, simultaneously to millions of isolated individuals, a totalizing ideology that appears to explain everything and offers membership in a powerful movement.
∴ Mass media combined with social atomization creates conditions uniquely favorable to totalitarian movements: isolated individuals, hungry for belonging, are offered an all-encompassing worldview that promises to make them part of something greater than themselves.

Arendt's argument adds an important dimension to Orwell's analysis. Orwell focuses on the content of propaganda — the lies, the slogans, the manipulation. Arendt focuses on the audience: why are people susceptible to propaganda in the first place? Her answer — that social isolation makes people hungry for the kind of belonging that ideological movements provide — has obvious implications for our present situation. If social media simultaneously increases social connection and deepens loneliness, as some researchers argue, it might be amplifying exactly the vulnerability that Arendt identified.

Case Study

Nazi Radio and the Volksempfänger (1933–1945)

When the National Socialist (Nazi) Party came to power in Germany in 1933, radio was still a relatively expensive medium. Nazi Propaganda Minister Joseph Goebbels immediately recognized its potential and launched production of the Volksempfänger — the "People's Receiver" — a cheap, standardized radio set designed to bring Nazi broadcasts into every German home. By 1939, Germany had the highest per-capita radio ownership in the world: over sixteen million sets, reaching approximately seventy percent of the population.

The Nazis used radio to broadcast Hitler's speeches live to the entire nation, to saturate daily life with Party music, news, and ideology, and to create the impression of a unified, enthusiastic national community. Crucially, the radios were designed to receive but not transmit — the audience could only listen, not respond. This was, as Orwell and Arendt would have predicted, a technology of one-way communication deployed in service of a regime that required passive acceptance, not active citizenship.

The Nazi radio program illustrates a structural feature of broadcast media that internet advocates often cite as a weakness: it is inherently one-to-many, not many-to-many. The question of whether social media's many-to-many structure is better or worse in practice is one we will examine in later chapters.

Thought Questions

  1. Arendt argues that loneliness makes people susceptible to totalitarian movements. If social media increases loneliness, does it increase this risk? What evidence bears on this question?
  2. Orwell thought the ultimate goal of propaganda was to destroy people's capacity to distinguish truth from falsehood. Does the term "post-truth" suggest this has happened, or is it an exaggeration?
  3. The Nazis used radio to create a sense of national unity and belonging. Is the sense of community created by online groups healthy, dangerous, or something in between?

Revolution 5: The World Wide Web

In 1989, a British physicist named Tim Berners-Lee, working at the European particle physics laboratory CERN in Switzerland, wrote a memo proposing a system for managing information across the lab's network of computers. His supervisor wrote "Vague but exciting" on the cover and returned it. Two years later, Berners-Lee had built the World Wide Web: a system of hyperlinked documents accessible through a common protocol and address system. He deliberately refused to patent his invention, releasing it as an open standard for the benefit of all humanity. By the mid-1990s, the Web was transforming global communication. By the 2000s, it had become the dominant medium of human expression.

The early web carried utopian overtones familiar from every previous information revolution. "Information wants to be free," declared the technologist Stewart Brand. The web would disintermediate — cut out the middlemen — between producers and consumers of information, empowering individuals and undermining institutional gatekeepers. The comparison with the printing press was everywhere. But if the first phase of the web resembled a printing press in the hands of everyone, the second phase — Web 2.0 and the rise of social media platforms — more closely resembled the consolidation of broadcast media into a few powerful hands.

Disintermediation

The removal of intermediaries (gatekeepers, middlemen) between producers and consumers. Each information revolution has promised disintermediation; each has been followed by reintermediation — the emergence of new intermediaries, often more powerful than those they replaced.

By 2010, a handful of American technology companies — Google, Facebook, Amazon, Apple — had become the new gatekeepers of global information. Their algorithms determined what news billions of people saw, what products they were shown, and what ideas were amplified or suppressed. The companies were driven primarily by advertising revenue, which depends on maximizing user engagement — and research has shown that engagement is maximized by content that produces strong emotional reactions, including outrage and fear. For example, YouTube's recommendation algorithm was documented in multiple studies to systematically guide viewers toward increasingly extreme content: a casual search for a mainstream political speech could, within a handful of recommended videos, lead toward fringe conspiracy channels — not because anyone programmed it to radicalize users, but because more extreme content generated more watch time. Facebook's internal researchers, whose findings were later leaked to Congress by whistleblower Frances Haugen in 2021, discovered that posts provoking "angry" reactions received five times the algorithmic weight of posts receiving a simple Like — making outrage the platform's most-rewarded currency.

The result is a communications environment whose basic structure none of the early web utopians predicted: an open network carrying traffic that is filtered and shaped by private, for-profit intermediaries whose business interests are not necessarily aligned with the public good. This is not unique to the web. The telegram was carried by companies with commercial interests. Radio and television were dominated by networks serving advertisers. The pattern of disintermediation followed by reintermediation is, at this point, one of the most reliable features of IT history.

Consider how consistent this pattern has been: the printing press disrupted the Church's monopoly on copying texts, but within decades secular printers and state censors had established new forms of control over what could be published. The telegraph disrupted the postal system, but within years Western Union had a near-monopoly on telegraph wires and used it to favor certain newspaper clients over others. Radio promised to democratize public discourse, but within two decades a small number of national networks — NBC, CBS, ABC — controlled what most Americans heard. The internet promised to disintermediate everything, and it did — but it reintermediated around Google, Facebook, and Amazon. The form of the intermediary changes; the fact of it does not.

Debate Position A Position B
Content moderation Maximize free expression; platforms should not censor Platforms must limit harmful content to protect users and democracy
Data privacy Users accept terms of service; data sharing enables services Users cannot meaningfully consent to surveillance at this scale
Platform responsibility Platforms are neutral conduits and should not be held liable for content Platforms actively curate and amplify content; they bear editorial responsibility
Algorithmic amplification Personalization improves relevance and user experience Engagement-optimized algorithms amplify outrage and misinformation

Case Study

The Arab Spring and Social Media (2010–2012)

Beginning in Tunisia in December 2010 and spreading across North Africa and the Middle East, the Arab Spring was a wave of popular uprisings against authoritarian governments that became closely associated with social media — particularly Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube. Activists used these platforms to organize protests, share video evidence of government abuses, and coordinate across geographic boundaries with a speed and scale that had not been possible before. The Tunisian and Egyptian governments fell within months.

The early coverage portrayed the Arab Spring as a vindication of the web's democratic promise: a "Facebook Revolution" or "Twitter Revolution" in which social media had put the tools of political organization in the hands of ordinary people, bypassing state control of broadcast media. By 2013, however, this optimism had faded. Several of the uprisings devolved into civil wars with enormous humanitarian costs. The regimes that survived adapted quickly, using the same social media platforms for surveillance, propaganda, and the identification of dissidents. In Syria, Egypt, and Bahrain, social media posts were used to track and arrest activists.

The Arab Spring illustrates both halves of the IT revolution pattern: the same platform that enabled unprecedented popular organization also became a tool of state repression. The technology was not neutral — but neither was it simply in service of power. It served whoever used it most effectively, which sometimes meant the people, and sometimes meant the state.

Thought Questions

  1. Tim Berners-Lee gave the Web to humanity as an open standard, refusing to patent it. Was this the right decision? What might have been different if the Web had been proprietary?
  2. The Arab Spring showed social media enabling both popular revolution and government repression. What does this suggest about claims that technology is inherently liberating?
  3. Each IT revolution has produced new gatekeepers to replace old ones. Is there any way to prevent this, or is it an iron law of information technology history?

The Pattern Across the Revolutions

Looking across five information technology revolutions, a pattern emerges that is striking in its consistency. Every revolution has promised liberation: writing would preserve wisdom, the printing press would spread it, the telegraph would unite humanity, broadcast media would educate the masses, and the internet would empower everyone equally. Every revolution has also enabled new forms of control: imperial bureaucracy, religious war, colonial coordination, totalitarian propaganda, and surveillance capitalism. In every case, both the optimists and the pessimists turned out to be right — about different things, or about the same things at different times.

The most important lesson of this history may be about the relationship between technology and power. Technology is not a neutral tool that societies simply pick up and use. It is developed within existing social structures, deployed by people with interests and agendas, and has effects that are shaped by who controls it, who has access to it, and what economic and political system it operates within. This is not a counsel of despair — it does not mean that technology always serves the powerful or that resistance is futile. The printing press really did spread literacy and challenge the Church's monopoly on knowledge. Social media really did enable ordinary people to organize and communicate in ways that authoritarian governments found difficult to stop, at least initially. But it does mean that asking "Is this technology good or bad?" is usually the wrong question. The right questions are: Who controls this technology? Who benefits? Who bears the costs? And what governance structures could make the distribution of benefits more just?

Table 1.3 — The Five Revolutions: Promises, Pessimists, and Outcomes
Revolution Optimist's Promise Pessimist's Warning What Actually Happened
Writing Preserve and spread knowledge (Theuth) Weaken memory and produce false wisdom (Socrates) Both: civilization and bureaucratic empire
Printing Press Democratize access to ideas (Luther) Spread dangerous ideas without expert mediation (Erasmus) Both: Scientific Revolution and religious wars
Distance Communication Unite humanity through free exchange (Mill) Serve whoever owns the infrastructure (Marx) Both: global connection and imperial coordination
Mass Media Educate and inform the masses Enable propaganda and destroy political thought (Orwell, Arendt) Both: public education and totalitarian propaganda
The Web Empower everyone, disintermediate gatekeepers Create new monopolies and surveillance systems Both: and the story is still being written

This table reveals something important about how to think about the upcoming topics in this course. Free speech, intellectual property, privacy, AI, and labor automation are not entirely new problems. They are new chapters in a long story. When we ask whether AI companies should be liable for harmful content generated by their systems, we are asking a version of the question that was asked about printing houses in the seventeenth century. When we debate whether to regulate social media algorithms, we are echoing debates about the regulation of telegraph monopolies in the nineteenth century. The specific technologies change. The fundamental questions about power, access, truth, and harm recur.

Summary

This chapter has introduced the history of IT ethics through the lens of five major information technology revolutions. In each case, we found both the optimists and the pessimists partially right — and the decisive factor was not the technology itself but who controlled it and toward what ends. The thinkers we have met — Socrates, Plato, Erasmus, Luther, Mill, Marx, Orwell, and Arendt — offer frameworks that remain powerful for analyzing contemporary technology, from content moderation and surveillance capitalism to artificial intelligence and algorithmic amplification. We will draw on all of them in the chapters ahead.

Key Points

  • Information technology is ancient: Writing, the printing press, the telegraph, and the internet are all IT, sharing structural features and raising structurally similar ethical questions.
  • Socrates's argument (in Plato's Phaedrus) is the first recorded technology ethics debate: writing produces the appearance of wisdom, not wisdom itself, because it cannot respond to questions or adapt to its audience.
  • The Luther-Erasmus debate established the enduring tension between free dissemination of ideas and responsible gatekeeping — a tension directly relevant to content moderation on social media today.
  • Mill saw communications technology as aligned with individual liberty and the free marketplace of ideas. Marx countered that technology serves whoever owns it — and that in capitalist societies, ownership is concentrated.
  • Orwell warned that state control of mass media could destroy the capacity to distinguish truth from falsehood. Arendt identified social atomization as the precondition that makes populations susceptible to totalitarian propaganda.
  • Disintermediation and reintermediation: Every IT revolution promises to cut out gatekeepers; every revolution produces new ones. The Web's promised disintermediation has been followed by the rise of a handful of platform monopolies.
  • The recurring pattern: Every information revolution promises liberation and enables new forms of control. Both optimists and pessimists are typically right — about different things, or the same things at different times.
  • The right questions about a technology are not "is it good or bad?" but: Who controls it? Who benefits? Who bears the costs? What governance could make it more just?
