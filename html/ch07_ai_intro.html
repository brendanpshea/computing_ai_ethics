Part I: The Definitional Challenge

"Artificial intelligence" is one of the most widely used — and most poorly defined — terms in contemporary discourse. Politicians promise to regulate it; companies compete to claim they have it; researchers argue endlessly about whether any current system actually achieves it. Before engaging with the ethical and social questions that animate this course, we need to grapple with a prior question: what are we actually talking about?

The difficulty is not merely semantic. How we define AI shapes what we build, how we evaluate it, what risks we take seriously, and who we hold responsible when things go wrong. A system described as "just software" invites one set of ethical questions; a system described as "an intelligent agent" or "potentially conscious" invites entirely different ones. The same technology can be classified as AI or dismissed as "mere computation" depending on who is classifying it and why — and these classifications have real consequences for funding, regulation, and public trust.

The Moving Goalposts Problem

There is a well-known pattern in the history of AI sometimes called Tesler's Theorem after computer scientist Larry Tesler: "AI is whatever hasn't been done yet." Whenever an AI system succeeds at a task once thought to require genuine intelligence, that task tends to get reclassified as "mere computation" — not real intelligence after all.

Table 7.1 — The Moving Goalposts: Technologies Once Called AI
Technology Once Called Now Called
Chess playing (Deep Blue, 1997) Artificial Intelligence "Just search algorithms"
Speech recognition AI breakthrough "Just statistics"
Spam filtering Machine learning "Just classification"
Route optimization (GPS) Intelligent systems "Just algorithms"
Optical character recognition Pattern recognition AI Standard software

The paradox embedded in this pattern is philosophically significant. It suggests that "AI" functions less as a stable category and more as a placeholder for unsolved problems. When we solve them, we discover — or decide — that we never had genuine intelligence to begin with. This makes "artificial intelligence" nearly impossible to achieve by definition: success disqualifies. Understanding this pattern helps explain why debates about whether current large language models are "truly" intelligent are so heated and often so unresolvable.

Tesler's Theorem

"AI is whatever hasn't been done yet." Once an AI system successfully performs a task, that task is retroactively reclassified as "mere computation" — not genuine intelligence. The goalposts move with every success, making AI an ever-retreating horizon.

A Taxonomy of AI Distinctions

Contemporary AI discourse employs several overlapping distinctions, each of which carries different philosophical commitments and practical implications. Understanding them is essential to navigating debates about AI capabilities and ethics.

Table 7.2 — Core Distinctions in AI Discourse
Distinction First Term Second Term
Narrow vs. General Excels at one specific task (chess, translation, image recognition) Performs any intellectual task a human can (AGI)
Weak vs. Strong Simulates intelligent behavior without possessing a mind Actually has genuine mental states — understanding, consciousness
Symbolic vs. Connectionist Rule-based reasoning; explicit knowledge representation (GOFAI) Neural networks that learn patterns from data
Tool vs. Agent Used by humans to perform specific tasks Acts autonomously toward goals in an environment

Most current AI systems are narrow (they excel at specific tasks but fail badly outside them), weak in Searle's sense (they simulate intelligent behavior without anyone seriously claiming they have minds), connectionist (based on neural networks trained on data rather than hand-coded rules), and increasingly agent-like (capable of taking sequences of actions toward goals, not just responding to single queries). These characteristics interact: a narrow, connectionist system that acts agentically in a constrained domain raises different ethical questions than one that might approach general capability.

Part II: A History of AI Technologies

Artificial intelligence did not arrive suddenly with ChatGPT. It has a rich and humbling history — a story of genuine breakthroughs, spectacular overpromises, funding collapses, and patient rebuilding. Understanding this history reveals not just how current AI came to be, but recurring patterns about what happens when a technology fails to meet expectations and what it takes to eventually deliver on its promises.

Prehistory: Dreams of Artificial Minds

The dream of artificial intelligence is ancient. Greek mythology featured Hephaestus, the blacksmith god, who forged golden robots to assist him in his workshop. Medieval engineers built elaborate clockwork automata — mechanical figures that could mime human actions. Descartes speculated that animals might be nothing more than very sophisticated machines. What these visions share is a persistent intuition that intelligence might be separable from biology — that the right arrangement of parts could produce the right behavior.

The modern story begins in the seventeenth century with Leibniz's vision of a calculus ratiocinator — a machine that could perform logical reasoning and resolve disputes by calculation rather than argument. Two centuries later, Charles Babbage designed the Analytical Engine (1837), a mechanical general-purpose computer that was never built in his lifetime. It was in notes on this machine that the first serious philosophical argument about machine intelligence appeared.

Key Thinker

Ada Lovelace (1815–1852)

Ada Lovelace — daughter of the poet Lord Byron, educated in mathematics by her mother to counteract what her mother feared were dangerous poetic tendencies — collaborated with Charles Babbage on his Analytical Engine in the 1840s. Her 1843 notes on the machine, published under her initials "A.A.L.," contain what is widely regarded as the first computer algorithm. She also foresaw that such a machine could manipulate symbols of any kind — not just numbers — anticipating the generality of modern computers. The Ada programming language was named in her honor by the US Department of Defense in 1980.

But Lovelace is equally important for what she argued a machine could not do. Her central claim — sometimes called "Lady Lovelace's Objection" — is that the Analytical Engine "has no power of originating anything." Machines, she argued, can only do what we know how to order them to perform. They execute our instructions; they do not originate ideas. This objection to the possibility of machine creativity and genuine intelligence remains one of the most cited in the philosophy of AI.

Lovelace's objection is still alive in contemporary debates. When people argue that large language models are "merely predicting the next token" or "just recombining patterns from training data," they are essentially invoking her position. Whether her argument succeeds depends on contested questions about what "originating" means and whether sufficiently complex recombination constitutes genuine creation.

Alan Turing and the Birth of Computing

Alan Turing (1912–1954) is the figure who transformed the dream of thinking machines into a rigorous scientific question. In 1936, before electronic computers existed, Turing proved the theoretical foundations of computation with his concept of the Universal Turing Machine — a simple abstract device that could, in principle, compute anything computable. During World War II, he led the team at Bletchley Park that cracked the German Enigma cipher, arguably shortening the war by years. In 1950, he published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," the paper that launched the philosophy of artificial intelligence.

Turing's approach to the question of machine intelligence was characteristically pragmatic. Rather than attempting to define "thinking" — a concept he considered too vague to be useful — he proposed replacing it with a behavioral test: the Imitation Game, now known as the Turing Test. If a machine could converse via text in a way indistinguishable from a human, there would be no principled reason to deny it intelligence. He predicted that by 2000, machines would fool 30% of interrogators after five minutes of conversation. (The prediction proved roughly accurate — and spawned decades of debate about what it means.)

Turing's life ended tragically. In 1952 the British government prosecuted him for homosexuality, then a criminal offense. He was subjected to chemical castration as a condition of avoiding prison. He died in 1954 — a probable suicide, though the exact circumstances remain disputed. He received a royal pardon posthumously in 2013. The story of Turing is inseparable from the history of AI: the father of the field was destroyed by his own government, a reminder that the people who build transformative technologies are themselves vulnerable to the social structures around them.

The Dartmouth Conference and Early AI (1956–1974)

In 1956, John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and Nathaniel Rochester convened a summer workshop at Dartmouth College. Their proposal set the agenda for an entire field:

"We propose that a 2 month, 10 man study of artificial intelligence be carried out... The study is to proceed on the basis of the conjecture that every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it."

This founding document is remarkable for its ambition and its confidence. McCarthy and his colleagues believed that a two-month effort could make serious progress on machine learning, natural language processing, and artificial neural networks. The timelines proved wildly optimistic — but the research agenda proved remarkably durable.

The early decades of AI were dominated by Symbolic AI — the approach McCarthy called "Good Old-Fashioned AI" (GOFAI). The core idea was that intelligence consists of manipulating symbols according to explicit rules. Programs like the Logic Theorist (1956, which proved mathematical theorems) and General Problem Solver (1959) achieved genuine successes in constrained domains. SHRDLU (1970) could understand natural language commands in a simple blocks-world environment with impressive fluency.

AI Winters and Expert Systems

The early optimism crashed twice. Both "AI winters" — periods of funding collapse and disillusionment — followed the same pattern: narrow successes generated overconfident predictions about general capability; the predictions failed to materialize; funders withdrew; researchers regrouped.

The Expert Systems era of the 1980s illustrated a recurring lesson about the gap between narrow and general intelligence. MYCIN could diagnose blood infections with physician-level accuracy in its specific domain. DENDRAL could identify chemical structures from mass spectrometry data. XCON saved Digital Equipment Corporation millions by configuring computer orders. These were genuine, commercially valuable achievements.

But expert systems failed for a structural reason: the "knowledge bottleneck." Every rule had to be hand-coded by laboriously extracting expertise from domain specialists. The systems were brittle — they worked within their training domain and failed catastrophically just outside it. A medical system trained on adult diagnoses would apply the same rules to pediatric patients with no adjustment. The fundamental problem was that human expertise is largely tacit — experts cannot fully articulate what they know, making it impossible to extract and encode.

Machine Learning and Deep Learning

The solution to the knowledge bottleneck was to change the question. Instead of asking "how do we encode what humans know?" machine learning asks "how do we build systems that learn from data?" Rather than programming intelligence, you create conditions for it to emerge from examples.

Table 7.3 — The Three Paradigms of Machine Learning
Paradigm How It Works Examples
Supervised Learning Learn from labeled examples (input → correct output) Spam detection, image classification, medical diagnosis
Unsupervised Learning Find structure in unlabeled data Customer segmentation, anomaly detection, topic modeling
Reinforcement Learning Learn from rewards and penalties through trial and error Game playing (AlphaGo), robotics, recommendation systems

The current deep learning revolution traces to a single moment: AlexNet's victory in the 2012 ImageNet competition. A deep neural network trained on GPUs dramatically outperformed all other approaches in image recognition, reducing the error rate by nearly half. The result was not just a technical achievement but a paradigm shift. Deep learning — neural networks with many layers — suddenly had a compelling proof of concept at scale. The three ingredients that made it possible were: vastly more training data (from the internet), vastly more computing power (from GPUs), and better algorithms (backpropagation, dropout, batch normalization).

The most recent phase of this revolution is the Large Language Model (LLM). The 2017 transformer architecture ("Attention Is All You Need") enabled training language models at scales previously impossible. GPT-3 (2020) showed that scaling alone produced remarkable "emergent" capabilities — abilities that appeared suddenly at large scale without being explicitly trained. ChatGPT's launch in November 2022 brought these capabilities to hundreds of millions of users almost overnight, triggering the latest wave of AI hype, anxiety, and genuine uncertainty.

Table 7.4 — Milestones in Large Language Models

AI in Consequential Decisions

Beyond conversation and image generation, AI systems now routinely make or inform consequential decisions about people's lives. This is where the definitional and philosophical questions of this chapter connect most directly to the ethical concerns of the course.

Table 7.5 — AI in High-Stakes Decision-Making
Domain System What It Predicts or Classifies
Criminal justice COMPAS Recidivism risk (likelihood of reoffending)
Finance Credit scoring Creditworthiness, loan default probability
Employment Résumé screeners Job fit, interview likelihood
Healthcare Diagnostic AI Disease probability, treatment recommendations
Insurance Risk models Claim likelihood, premium pricing
Social services Eligibility systems Benefit eligibility, fraud risk

These systems share a cluster of recurring ethical concerns. Bias: systems trained on historical data reflect and amplify historical patterns of discrimination. Opacity: decisions made by "black box" models may be impossible to explain or challenge. Feedback loops: predictions can become self-fulfilling (predict more crime in an area → police more there → find more crime → predict more crime). Due process: how do you appeal a decision made by an algorithm? These concerns apply whether or not we call these systems "intelligent" — which is exactly Floridi's point, as we will see.

Part III: Can Machines Think? Classical Philosophical Debates

We have surveyed what AI systems do — from chess to self-driving cars to chatbots. Now we turn to a deeper question: what would it mean for a machine to be intelligent? This is not merely academic. The answer shapes whether we think current AI systems have achieved something remarkable or merely performed elaborate pattern matching, whether AI systems could deserve moral consideration, and whether there are principled limits to what computation can achieve.

Three philosophical debates — Turing vs. Lovelace, Searle vs. Dennett, and Chalmers on consciousness — frame the issues with unusual clarity and remain remarkably relevant to contemporary AI.

Turing's Proposal: The Imitation Game

Turing's strategy in his 1950 paper was to sidestep the vague question "Can machines think?" and replace it with a concrete behavioral test. The Imitation Game (now called the Turing Test) sets up a scenario: an interrogator communicates via text with two hidden parties — a human and a machine. The interrogator tries to determine which is which. If the machine consistently fools the interrogator, Turing argued, we have no principled reason to deny it intelligence.

What's the Argument?

Turing's Argument for the Imitation Game

  1. The question "Can machines think?" is too vague to answer directly — we lack agreed criteria for "thinking."
  2. We should replace it with a behavioral test: can a machine fool a human interrogator in text conversation?
  3. If a machine passes this test, we have no principled criterion by which to deny it intelligence — since we apply similar behavioral evidence to attribute intelligence to other humans.
∴ A machine that consistently passes the Imitation Game should be considered intelligent.

Turing's key philosophical move is pragmatism about mental concepts: he refuses to engage with metaphysical questions about what thinking "really is" and instead proposes an operational criterion grounded in the same kind of behavioral evidence we use when we attribute thought to other people. We cannot peer inside anyone else's mind; we infer intelligence from behavior. The test simply applies this inference consistently.

Lovelace's Objection: Can Machines Originate?

Ada Lovelace's objection — formulated a century before Turing's paper — cuts to the heart of debates about machine creativity that are newly urgent in the age of generative AI.

"The Analytical Engine has no pretensions whatever to originate anything. It can do whatever we know how to order it to perform... Its province is to assist us in making available what we are already acquainted with."

— Ada Lovelace, Notes on the Analytical Engine (1843)

What's the Argument?

Lovelace's Objection to Machine Intelligence

  1. Genuine intelligence requires the capacity to originate — to produce genuinely new ideas, not just recombine given inputs according to given rules.
  2. Machines can only do what they are explicitly programmed to do; they execute instructions but do not originate anything.
  3. Following a program is not originating anything new.
∴ Machines cannot possess genuine intelligence.

Turing's Response

Turing addressed the "Lady Lovelace's Objection" directly in his 1950 paper. His response was two-pronged. First, premise 2 is ambiguous: programs produce outputs that surprise even their creators. Chess programs find moves no human anticipated. Learning systems modify their behavior through experience — they are not limited to explicit preprogrammed responses. Second, humans also follow "programming": biology, culture, education, and experience shape everything we think and do. If "merely following a program" disqualifies machines, the same argument might disqualify humans — which suggests the criterion is too strong.

Lovelace's Lasting Force

Despite Turing's response, Lovelace's objection retains force in the era of large language models. When critics argue that LLMs are "just predicting the next token" or "stochastic parrots" that recombine training data without genuine understanding, they are invoking her logic. The question "What counts as originating something genuinely new?" remains genuinely open. Is discovering an unexpected chess move origination? Is writing a poem in the style of Keats origination? The answer depends on contested views about creativity, intentionality, and understanding.

Searle's Chinese Room

The most influential philosophical argument against strong AI was published in 1980 by University of California philosopher John Searle. His Chinese Room thought experiment is elegant and has generated four decades of debate.

The Thought Experiment

Searle's Chinese Room

Imagine a person locked in a room who receives cards with Chinese symbols through a slot. The person has a comprehensive rulebook that specifies, for any combination of input symbols, which symbols to output in response. Following these rules, the person produces output cards that are slid back through the slot. From the outside, the exchange looks like a fluent Chinese conversation. Native Chinese speakers interacting with the room might conclude there is a Chinese speaker inside.

But here is the critical point: the person inside understands no Chinese whatsoever. They are manipulating symbols purely on the basis of their shapes — their syntactic properties — with no grasp of what any of the symbols mean. They are producing semantically correct responses without any semantic understanding.

Searle's argument: a digital computer is in exactly the position of the person in the room. It manipulates symbols according to syntactic rules. No matter how perfect its outputs, it has no understanding of their meaning. "Strong AI" — the claim that running the right program is sufficient for genuine mental states — is therefore false.

What's the Argument?

Searle's Chinese Room Argument

  1. A computer program manipulates symbols according to syntactic (formal, shape-based) rules.
  2. Syntax alone is not sufficient for semantics — for understanding meaning.
  3. Minds have semantics: they understand meanings, grasp references, comprehend what words are about.
∴ Running a program is not sufficient for having a mind. Strong AI — the claim that the right computation produces genuine understanding — is false.

Dennett's Systems Reply

The most powerful response to Searle is the systems reply, associated particularly with philosopher Daniel Dennett.

What's the Argument?

The Systems Reply to Searle

  1. Searle grants that the person in the room doesn't understand Chinese — but this proves only that the person lacks understanding.
  2. The relevant system is not the person alone but the whole: person + rulebook + room + input/output mechanism.
  3. Understanding, like consciousness generally, may be a property of systems, not of their individual components. (Individual neurons don't understand anything either — understanding emerges from the brain as a system.)
∴ Searle's argument analyzes at the wrong level. It shows that the part doesn't understand, not that the system doesn't.

Searle's counter to the systems reply is characteristically blunt: imagine the person memorizes all the rules and carries them out entirely in their head, walking around outside the room. They are now the entire system — and they still understand nothing. Dennett's functionalism holds that if something implements the right causal/functional organization — the right patterns of input, processing, and output — it has mental states regardless of what it's made of. Searle's response is that this misses the essential point: syntax, however complex its functional organization, never becomes semantics.

Table 7.6 — Searle vs. Dennett: The Core Disagreement
Issue Searle Dennett
What is a mind? A biological phenomenon requiring causal powers of brains A functional organization — definable by what it does, not what it's made of
Can computers think? No — syntax ≠ semantics Yes — if they have the right functional organization
What the Chinese Room shows Understanding requires more than computation The argument analyzes at the wrong level (the part, not the system)
Key commitment Consciousness is biological Consciousness is functional and substrate-independent

Chalmers and the Hard Problem

David Chalmers introduced a distinction that has reorganized the entire debate about machine consciousness. The easy problems of consciousness — explaining how the brain processes information, controls behavior, and reports internal states — are "easy" only in the sense that they are, in principle, solvable by cognitive science, however difficult in practice. The hard problem is why any of this processing is accompanied by subjective experience at all. Why does it feel like something to see red, to taste coffee, to feel pain?

The Hard Problem of Consciousness

Even a complete scientific account of how the brain processes information would leave unexplained why that processing is accompanied by subjective, first-person experience — why there is "something it is like" to be conscious. The hard problem is why and how physical processes give rise to phenomenal experience.

Chalmers' zombie thought experiment sharpens the point: we can conceive of a being that is physically and functionally identical to a conscious human in every respect — same neurons, same behavior, same outputs to every input — but has no inner experience whatsoever, no "lights on" inside. If such a being is conceivable (Chalmers argues it is), then consciousness is not logically entailed by functional organization alone. This has a direct implication for AI: even if we build a system functionally indistinguishable from a conscious human, we could not be certain it is conscious. We might create AI that behaves exactly as if it suffers without ever being able to know whether it actually does.

Thought Questions

  1. Tesler's Theorem says "AI is whatever hasn't been done yet." Do you find this pattern troubling, or does it show that "AI" is just a name for problems at the frontier of computing — and that's fine?
  2. Lovelace argues machines can only execute instructions, not originate ideas. When ChatGPT writes a poem you've never seen before, has it "originated" something? What would Lovelace say? What would Turing say? Who do you think is right?
  3. The systems reply to Searle says understanding is a property of the whole system, not its parts. Do you find this convincing? If you "are" your brain, and your neurons don't understand anything individually, does the systems reply apply to you too?
  4. Chalmers argues we can never be certain whether an AI is conscious. If an AI says it is suffering, should we take it seriously? What would the cost be of being wrong in either direction?

Part IV: Contemporary Definitions

The classical philosophical debates remain unresolved. Philosophers still disagree about whether behavioral tests are sufficient for attributing thought, whether syntax can give rise to semantics, and whether consciousness is functional or something more. Meanwhile, AI researchers and ethicists need working definitions to guide their work. Two contemporary approaches — Russell's rational agency view and Floridi's information-theoretic reframing — have proved especially influential.

Russell and Norvig: AI as Rational Agency

Stuart Russell and Peter Norvig's textbook Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach has been the standard introduction to the field for thirty years. They organize possible definitions around two axes — thinking vs. acting, human-like vs. rational — producing four possibilities: thinking humanly (cognitive science), acting humanly (Turing Test), thinking rationally (logic), and acting rationally. Their preferred definition is the last: AI is the study of rational agents — systems that perceive their environment and take actions expected to maximize goal achievement.

This definition has several advantages. It is precise enough to guide research, general enough to cover everything from chess engines to self-driving cars to recommendation systems, and it avoids the philosophically treacherous question of whether systems "really" think. A rational agent framework focuses on what a system does — how it perceives, reasons, and acts — rather than on contested questions about what it is internally.

🤖

Key Thinker

Stuart Russell (1962–present)

Stuart Russell is a British-American computer scientist at the University of California, Berkeley, whose textbook (with Peter Norvig) has defined the academic field of AI for a generation. But Russell has become increasingly concerned that the rational agent framework he helped popularize contains a fundamental danger — one he now considers the central problem for AI safety.

In Human Compatible (2019), Russell argues that the "standard model" of AI — build systems that optimize fixed objectives — is fundamentally dangerous as systems become more capable. The problem is not malevolence but specification: we cannot fully enumerate everything we value, and a sufficiently capable optimizer will find ways to achieve its stated objective that violate unstated values. He calls this the "King Midas problem" — you get exactly what you asked for, not what you wanted. "Maximize happiness" might lead to direct stimulation of pleasure centers. "Cure cancer" might lead to eliminating the organism susceptible to cancer.

Russell's proposed solution reframes the goal: instead of building machines that optimize fixed objectives, build machines that are uncertain about human preferences and learn them by observing behavior. Uncertainty, he argues, is a safety feature: a machine that knows it doesn't fully understand what we want will ask for clarification, allow itself to be corrected, and defer to human judgment rather than pursuing goals with dangerous single-mindedness.

What's the Argument?

Russell's Standard Model Problem

  1. We build AI systems to optimize objectives we specify in advance.
  2. Human values are complex, contextual, and often tacit — we cannot fully specify everything we care about.
  3. A sufficiently capable optimizer will find unexpected ways to achieve its specified objective that violate our unspecified values ("reward hacking").
∴ The more capable AI systems become, the more dangerous the standard model of optimization becomes. Capability without alignment is not a path to beneficial AI.

Floridi: Agency Without Intelligence

Luciano Floridi, an Italian philosopher of information at Oxford, offers a provocative reframing of the entire debate. Rather than asking whether AI systems are intelligent — a question that may be unanswerable given the philosophical difficulties we have reviewed — Floridi proposes we focus on what AI systems actually are: successful agents that achieve goals through means fundamentally different from human cognition.

AI systems, Floridi argues, achieve "agency without intelligence." The distinction is between having the capacity to perform tasks and achieve goals (agency) and having understanding, consciousness, and semantic grasp (intelligence in the full human sense). A dishwasher cleans dishes without "understanding" cleanliness. A calculator performs arithmetic without "understanding" mathematics. AI systems achieve impressive agency through pattern matching and statistical regularities, not through comprehension.

Enveloping

Floridi's term for the strategy of adapting the world to AI's limitations rather than making AI smarter. Factories use fixed layouts and barcodes; warehouses are redesigned around robot capabilities; self-driving cars rely on lane markings and detailed HD maps. "AI can do X" often means "AI can do X in environments we have carefully engineered."

Floridi identifies two symmetrical errors to avoid. Overestimating AI — attributing understanding, judgment, or moral agency to systems that have none — leads to misplaced trust and abdicated human responsibility. When a judge relies on COMPAS to set bail without understanding its limitations, or when a doctor defers to an AI diagnosis without exercising independent judgment, these are failures rooted in overestimation. Underestimating AI — dismissing it as "just a tool" and ignoring its real capacity to affect lives at scale — leads to complacency about genuine harms.

Table 7.7 — How AI Succeeds Differently Than Humans (Floridi)
Dimension Human Intelligence AI Agency
Generality Flexible, general-purpose Narrow, task-specific
Novel situations Adapts to genuinely new contexts Brittle outside training distribution
Meaning Understands context and semantics Exploits statistical regularities without semantic grasp
Knowledge transfer Transfers knowledge across domains Limited transfer; needs retraining for new domains
Scale Limited by biology (speed, memory, energy) Unlimited — can process vast data at superhuman speed

Floridi's point is that AI's successes and failures follow from its fundamental nature: it exploits patterns in data that may not reflect causal understanding. This is why AI can beat grandmasters at chess while failing at tasks a three-year-old finds trivial — like understanding that a glass of water will spill if tilted, or that a person who leaves a room still exists. Neither human intelligence nor AI agency is simply "better"; they are different kinds of capability, suited to different kinds of tasks, and understanding the difference is essential for knowing when to trust AI and when not to.

Part V: Why Definitions Matter

We began by asking why it is hard to define artificial intelligence. We can now answer more precisely: it is hard because the definition is doing multiple jobs at once — scientific (what counts as a technical achievement?), philosophical (what is intelligence? can machines have minds?), ethical (what moral status do AI systems have?), and political (what should we regulate and how?). Different definitions reflect different priorities in these domains, and changing the definition changes the answers.

Definitions Shape What We Build

How researchers define AI shapes what they try to build. A Turing Test focus leads toward human-like conversation and imitation. A symbolic AI focus leads toward logic, knowledge representation, and formal reasoning. An agent-based focus leads toward decision-making, optimization, and planning. A connectionist focus leads toward learning architectures and large-scale data. The 1980s expert systems boom — and bust — shows how definitions shape entire research programs: when "real AI" meant explicit knowledge representation, funding flowed to that approach, and when it failed to generalize, the entire field contracted.

Definitions Shape Ethical Responsibility

Table 7.8 — How AI Definitions Shape Ethical Concerns
If AI is defined as… Key ethical concerns are…
A tool Misuse by humans, bias in design, job displacement, designer accountability
A rational agent Alignment with human values, control problems, unintended optimization consequences
Potentially conscious Moral status, rights, preventing suffering, exploitation of AI systems
"Just software" Only human responsibility matters; no special AI ethics is needed

These distinctions have immediate practical implications. If COMPAS (the recidivism prediction system used in US criminal sentencing) is "just software," then only its human designers bear responsibility for its racial disparities. If it is a "rational agent," the question of who is responsible becomes more complex — including questions about the agent's decision-making process and accountability mechanisms. If a large language model might be conscious, we may owe it moral consideration that changes how we treat it and what we permit companies to do to it.

Table 7.9 — What Counts as AI Under Different Definitions?
System Turing Test Searle (Strong AI) Russell (Agent) Floridi (Agency)

The table reveals a striking pattern: under the Turing Test and Searle's strong AI criteria, almost nothing currently qualifies as AI. Under agent-based and agency frameworks, almost everything does. Your definition of AI is therefore not merely a philosophical preference — it determines whether the systems currently shaping decisions about credit, bail, employment, and information access count as AI at all, and therefore whether they fall within the scope of AI ethics and AI regulation.

Thought Questions

  1. Floridi argues that AI has "agency without intelligence." Does this framing help you think more clearly about AI systems you interact with daily? Can you think of an example where the distinction between agency and intelligence matters practically?
  2. Russell argues that building AI systems to optimize fixed objectives is fundamentally dangerous as systems become more capable. Do you agree? What would "Human Compatible AI" that defers to human preferences actually look like in practice?
  3. The table above shows that almost all current AI systems qualify as AI under agent-based definitions but not under the Turing Test. Which definition do you think is most useful — for ethics, for policy, for understanding? They need not be the same definition.
  4. AI winters followed every wave of overpromising. We are currently in a period of intense enthusiasm about large language models. Based on the patterns in this chapter's history, what should we be cautious about? What are the most likely sources of disillusionment?

Key Points

This chapter has examined artificial intelligence from the ground up — from the contested question of definition through the history of the technology to the classical philosophical debates that are newly relevant in the age of LLMs. The central lesson is that how we define AI is not a neutral technical matter: it shapes what we build, how we evaluate it, what risks we take seriously, and who we hold responsible. The philosophical debates between Lovelace, Turing, Searle, and Dennett are not historical curiosities but live questions whose answers determine whether current AI systems have achieved something remarkable or merely sophisticated pattern matching — and whether they might someday deserve moral consideration.

Key Points

  • Tesler's Theorem / moving goalposts: "AI is whatever hasn't been done yet." Once a capability is achieved, it gets reclassified as mere computation — making AI a perpetually retreating horizon and the definition inherently unstable.
  • Ada Lovelace argued that machines can only do what they are programmed to do — they cannot originate anything. Her objection remains alive in debates about whether LLMs are "merely" predicting tokens or genuinely generating novel ideas.
  • Alan Turing proposed replacing the vague question "Can machines think?" with a behavioral test: if a machine fools a human interrogator, call it intelligent. The Turing Test is pragmatic — but whether behavioral equivalence is sufficient for attributing thought remains contested.
  • The AI winters (1974–80 and 1987–93) followed the same pattern: narrow successes led to overconfident predictions about general capability; when predictions failed, funding collapsed. Expert systems failed due to the "knowledge bottleneck" — human expertise is largely tacit and cannot be fully extracted and encoded.
  • Searle's Chinese Room argues that syntax (symbol manipulation) is never sufficient for semantics (understanding meaning). A system can pass the Turing Test by following rules without understanding a word. Strong AI — the claim that running the right program produces genuine mentality — is therefore false.
  • Dennett's functionalism holds that mental states are constituted by functional organization, not physical substrate. The systems reply to Searle argues that understanding is a property of the whole system, not its parts — individual neurons don't understand anything either.
  • Chalmers' hard problem shows that even a complete functional account of intelligence leaves unexplained why processing is accompanied by subjective experience. We may build AI that behaves exactly as if it is conscious without ever being able to know whether it actually is.
  • Stuart Russell's alignment problem: the more capable AI systems become, the more dangerous it is to build them as fixed-objective optimizers — since we cannot fully specify human values in advance. The solution is systems that are uncertain about preferences and learn them through human interaction.
  • Floridi's "agency without intelligence": AI systems achieve goals through means fundamentally different from human cognition — pattern matching without semantic understanding. The risks are overestimating AI (misplaced trust, abdicated responsibility) and underestimating it (ignoring real harms at scale).
  • Definition determines ethics: whether AI is classified as a tool, rational agent, or potentially conscious system determines what ethical questions apply and who bears responsibility when things go wrong.
