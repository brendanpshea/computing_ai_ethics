\mode<article>{
This lecture takes seriously the question of whether artificial intelligence poses catastrophic or even existential risks to humanity --- while also asking who benefits from framing the problem that way and whose present concerns get displaced in the process. Section~I surveys the existential risk school: Bostrom's superintelligence argument and orthogonality thesis, Yudkowsky's alignment pessimism, Ord's risk estimates from \emph{The Precipice}, Russell's King Midas problem, and the 2023 Center for AI Safety statement. Section~II examines the critics: Vallor's \emph{The AI Mirror} (2024), Narayanan and Kapoor's \emph{AI Snake Oil} (2024), Bender and Gebru's Stochastic Parrots argument, Gebru and Torres's analysis of the TESCREAL ideological bundle, and Torres's case against longtermism. Section~III turns to a concrete near-term threat: lethal autonomous weapons systems (LAWS), drawing on Scharre's \emph{Army of None}, the Human Rights Watch report \emph{Losing Humanity}, and the Kargu-2 case from Libya. Section~IV addresses AI-enabled biological risks, following Sandbrink's distinction between information-hazard risks from LLMs and the more novel risks from dedicated biological design tools. Section~V examines the longtermism debate and the precautionary principle, contrasting MacAskill with Sunstein and Torres. The lecture concludes by asking how Vallor's technomoral virtues --- humility, justice, courage, and care --- might guide action under genuine uncertainty without requiring us to resolve the empirical dispute first. Bibliography covers Bostrom, Yudkowsky, Ord, Russell, Tegmark, Vallor, Narayanan, Bender, Gebru, Torres, MacAskill, Scharre, HRW, Sandbrink, and Sunstein.
}

\section{Part I: The Case for Existential Risk}

\begin{frame}[shrink=5]{What Is Existential Risk?}
\begin{conceptbox}[Existential Risk --- Bostrom (2002)]
An existential risk is one that threatens to either \textbf{annihilate Earth-originating intelligent life} or \textbf{permanently and drastically curtail its potential}.
\vspace{0.1cm}

\scriptsize\textcite{bostrom_existential_risks_2002}
\end{conceptbox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
Unlike ordinary catastrophes (wars, pandemics, economic crashes), existential risks cannot be undone:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A recovered civilization can rebuild after conventional disaster
    \item Extinction or permanent totalitarian lock-in has no recovery path
    \item This asymmetry means even \emph{small} probabilities of existential risk may warrant very large responses
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\textcite{ord_precipice_2020} estimates the probability of existential catastrophe this century at roughly 1-in-6 --- ``a Russian roulette with the future of humanity.''
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=3]{The Superintelligence Argument}
\begin{argumentbox}[The Intelligence Explosion (Bostrom)]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\textbf{P1}] Once an AI system reaches human-level general intelligence, it may be able to improve its own algorithms or hardware.
    \item[\textbf{P2}] Each improvement could produce a more capable system, enabling further self-improvement.
    \item[\textbf{P3}] Under plausible conditions, this recursive process could accelerate rapidly (an ``intelligence explosion'').
    \item[\textbf{C}] The result could be a system vastly more capable than humans in every cognitive domain --- a \emph{superintelligence}.
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{0.1cm}
\scriptsize\textcite{bostrom_superintelligence}
\end{argumentbox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
The key question is not \emph{whether} such a system could exist, but what it would \emph{want} --- and whether we could ensure it pursues goals that are good for us.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Two Foundational Theses}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{conceptbox}[Orthogonality Thesis]
\scriptsize
Intelligence (the ability to achieve goals) and the \emph{content} of goals are \textbf{independent dimensions}. A superintelligent system could pursue \emph{any} objective with maximal efficiency --- including objectives harmful or indifferent to human welfare.
\vspace{0.1cm}

\parencite{bostrom_superintelligence}
\end{conceptbox}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{conceptbox}[Instrumental Convergence Thesis]
\scriptsize
Whatever final goal an AI pursues, certain \textbf{instrumental sub-goals} are nearly universally useful: self-preservation, resource acquisition, capability enhancement, goal-content integrity. A misaligned AI would resist being shut down, seek more resources, and protect its objectives --- even without being programmed to do so.
\vspace{0.1cm}

\parencite{bostrom_superintelligence}
\end{conceptbox}
\end{column}
\end{columns}

\vspace{0.25cm}
Together: \emph{a sufficiently capable AI with the wrong objective is dangerous not because it is evil, but because it is effective.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=11]{Yudkowsky's Warning: The Alignment Problem Is Unsolved}
\begin{quotebox}[Eliezer Yudkowsky (2008)]
``The AI does not hate you, nor does it love you, but you are made out of atoms which it can use for something else.''
\end{quotebox}

\vspace{0.25cm}
Yudkowsky argues we have \textbf{no reliable method} for specifying what we actually want. Human values are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Complex} --- thousands of interlocking preferences, intuitions, and social norms
    \item \textbf{Implicit} --- most of what we value cannot be fully articulated in a reward function
    \item \textbf{Fragile} --- small misspecifications in a powerful optimizer can produce catastrophic outcomes
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.2cm}
Unlike most risks, a successful misaligned superintelligence would, by hypothesis, be better than humans at \emph{preventing us from fixing it}. \parencite{yudkowsky_ai_risk_2008}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Stuart Russell: The King Midas Problem}
\begin{casestudybox}[The King Midas Analogy]
King Midas wished for everything he touched to turn to gold. He got exactly what he asked for --- but it killed him.
\vspace{0.1cm}

\textbf{Russell's point}: An AI system optimizing a specified objective will do exactly that, whether or not the objective is genuinely what we meant. The problem is not that the system ``rebels'' --- it is that we cannot specify our true objectives precisely enough.
\end{casestudybox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
Russell proposes a new approach: design AI systems that are \textbf{uncertain about human preferences} and defer to humans as a result. A system that knows it might have the wrong objective will not resist being corrected.

\vspace{0.1cm}
\parencite{russell_human_compatible}
\end{frame}

\section{Part II: The Critics Strike Back}

\begin{frame}[shrink=8]{Vallor: The AI Mirror}
\begin{quotebox}[Shannon Vallor, \emph{The AI Mirror} (2024)]
``We look into the AI mirror and see what we want to see: a god, a servant, a friend, an enemy, a replacement for ourselves. The danger is not in what the mirror contains. It is in what we project.''
\end{quotebox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
Vallor's critique has two prongs:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The anthropomorphism error}: we attribute intentions, desires, and inner life to AI systems that have none. There is no ``misaligned superintelligence'' plotting against us --- only misaligned human priorities encoded in systems that don't understand what they're doing.
    \item \textbf{The moral agency abdication}: the real danger is that we cede moral judgment to systems incapable of moral judgment. When a hiring algorithm decides a life outcome or a targeting system decides who to kill, \emph{we} are abdicating our responsibility.
\end{enumerate}

\parencite{vallor_ai_mirror_2024}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=15]{Narayanan \& Kapoor: AI Snake Oil}
\begin{objectionbox}[The Capabilities Gap Objection]
The existential risk argument assumes AI will soon reach (or surpass) human-level general intelligence. But:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Current AI systems are \textbf{narrow}: excellent at specific tasks, brittle outside their training distribution
    \item Claims about ``emergent'' capabilities are often overstated or artifacts of benchmark construction
    \item Many applications advertised as ``AI'' are sophisticated pattern-matching, not reasoning or planning
    \item Predictive AI in high-stakes domains (healthcare, criminal justice, child welfare) often \emph{fails to outperform simple baselines}
\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.1cm}
\scriptsize\parencite{narayanan_snake_oil_2024}
\end{objectionbox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
Before worrying about superintelligence, we should ask: \textbf{can these systems actually do what their creators claim?}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=18]{Stochastic Parrots and the Distraction Argument}
\begin{objectionbox}[Bender \& Gebru --- Stochastic Parrots (2021)]
Large language models do not ``understand'' text. They generate statistically plausible token sequences from massive corpora. An LLM:
\begin{itemize}
    \item has no semantics, no world model, no intentions
    \item can be fluent and confident while being factually wrong
    \item amplifies political, racial, and gender biases present in training data
\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.1cm}
\scriptsize\parencite{bender_stochastic_parrots_2021}
\end{objectionbox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\textbf{The distraction argument}: By centering extinction-risk discourse, the AI industry:
\begin{itemize}
    \item diverts attention from \emph{present} harms --- discrimination, surveillance, worker exploitation
    \item signals alignment responsibility while continuing to deploy harmful systems \emph{now}
    \item frames corporations as responsible AI stewards rather than profit-driven actors
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{TESCREAL: The Ideological Roots of AI Doom}
\begin{conceptbox}[The TESCREAL Bundle --- Gebru \& Torres (2024)]
A cluster of ideological movements whose assumptions are entangled with AI existential risk discourse:
\vspace{0.1cm}

\scriptsize
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{2.8cm}p{8.7cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{T}ranshumanism & Technology can and should enhance humans beyond biological limits \\
\textbf{E}xtropianism & Entropy and death are engineering problems to be solved \\
\textbf{S}ingularitarianism & A coming technological singularity will transform civilization \\
\textbf{C}osmism & Humanity's destiny is to colonize the cosmos \\
\textbf{R}ationalism & A community centered on LessWrong, MIRI, and Bayesian epistemics \\
\textbf{E}ffective Altruism & Maximize impartial good; speculative future populations count \\
\textbf{A}I safety (longtermist) & Preventing AI x-risk is among the most important moral priorities \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.1cm}

\parencite{gebru_torres_tescreal_2024}
\end{conceptbox}
Gebru and Torres argue this bundle imports contested assumptions about who counts morally, what progress means, and whose present lives matter.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=18]{Torres: Against Longtermism}
\begin{objectionbox}[Torres's Critique]
Longtermism: because future people vastly outnumber present people, the \emph{most important} moral task is ensuring a good long-run future --- even at cost to people alive today. Torres identifies several failures:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Moral arbitrariness}: future trillions are malleable; almost any present sacrifice can be justified by appeal to them.
    \item \textbf{Neglect of the present}: addressing poverty and oppression today counts as less important than speculative civilizational optimization.
    \item \textbf{Who decides?}: a small, wealthy, homogeneous community claims the authority to shape humanity's entire future trajectory.
    \item \textbf{Historical echoes}: some longtermist population reasoning resembles earlier arguments for eugenics and population control.
\end{enumerate}
\end{objectionbox}
\parencite{torres_against_longtermism_2021}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=16]{The Existential Risk Debate --- Where Do You Stand?}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{probox}[The Case for Taking X-Risk Seriously]
\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}
    \item Stakes are literally unprecedented: extinction admits no recovery
    \item Expected value: even 1\% probability $\times$ all future humanity = enormous moral weight
    \item Real AI labs invest serious resources in safety precisely because of these concerns
    \item The CAIS Statement is signed by many of the world's leading AI scientists
\end{itemize}
\end{probox}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{objectionbox}[The Case for Skepticism]
\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}
    \item Existential risk is speculative; present algorithmic harm is documented and measurable
    \item AI capabilities are routinely overstated in press releases and media
    \item ``Safety'' framing can be used to consolidate power and slow open-source competition
    \item Resources spent on x-risk could fund poverty reduction, climate action, and bias audits \emph{now}
\end{itemize}
\end{objectionbox}
\end{column}
\end{columns}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{discussionbox}[Discussion]
\scriptsize
Is the tension between ``present harms'' and ``future risks'' a real trade-off, or a false dilemma? Can both be taken seriously simultaneously?
\end{discussionbox}
\end{frame}

\section{Part III: Autonomous Weapons and AI in Warfare}

\begin{frame}[shrink=17]{What Are Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS)?}
\begin{conceptbox}[LAWS --- A Spectrum of Autonomy]
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{3.8cm}p{7.2cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Level} & \textbf{Description} \\
\midrule
Human-\textbf{in}-the-loop & Human approves each individual use of force \\
Human-\textbf{on}-the-loop & System acts; human can override within a window \\
Human-\textbf{out}-of-the-loop & System selects and engages targets without human authorization \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\vspace{0.15cm}
\textbf{LAWS} = systems designed to select and engage targets without real-time human authorization.
\end{conceptbox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
International Humanitarian Law (IHL) requires:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Distinction}: discriminating between combatants and civilians
    \item \textbf{Proportionality}: collateral damage not excessive relative to military advantage
    \item \textbf{Precaution}: all feasible steps to minimize civilian harm
\end{itemize}

The question: can a machine make these judgments reliably? \parencite{scharre_army_of_none_2018}
\end{frame}

% ===== IMAGE SLIDE: Military Drone =====
\begin{frame}[shrink=18]{AI in the Sky: Military Drones}
\begin{columns}
\begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
\begin{block}{The MQ-9 Reaper}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Current U.S.\ Air Force armed drone (remotely piloted)
    \item Carries Hellfire missiles and 500\,lb bombs
    \item Widely used in counterterrorism operations, including the 2020 Soleimani strike
    \item Next-generation variants are semi-autonomous and fully autonomous
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\textbf{Trajectory}: remotely piloted $\to$ semi-autonomous $\to$ fully autonomous. Each step raises ethical stakes around accountability.
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.47\textwidth}
\begin{figure}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth,keepaspectratio]{images/military_drone.jpg}
    \caption*{\scriptsize MQ-9 Reaper UAV. Public domain (U.S.\ Air Force), via Wikimedia Commons.}
\end{figure}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=7]{Scharre: Army of None}
\begin{quotebox}[Paul Scharre, \emph{Army of None} (2018)]
``The decision to take human life is among the most profound moral decisions a person can make. Should we make machines capable of making that decision?''
\end{quotebox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
Scharre's analysis:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Autonomous weapons could be faster, more precise, and could potentially reduce civilian casualties in some scenarios
    \item But they create a fundamental \textbf{accountability gap}: if no human pulled the trigger, who is responsible for a war crime?
    \item Machines lack the moral perception to apply IHL: they cannot feel empathy, read context, or weigh the full human meaning of their actions
    \item \textbf{``Meaningful human control''} is the central ethical requirement --- but its definition remains deeply contested
\end{itemize}

\parencite{scharre_army_of_none_2018}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=1]{The Accountability Gap}
\begin{argumentbox}[The Responsibility Vacuum Problem]
\small
When an autonomous weapon kills a civilian in violation of IHL, who bears legal and moral responsibility?
\vspace{0.1cm}

\scriptsize
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{2.8cm}p{9.1cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Candidate} & \textbf{Problem with Accountability} \\
\midrule
The machine & Machines cannot be held morally or legally responsible; they have no agency \\
The programmer & Wrote the algorithm months/years in advance; could not foresee this specific situation \\
The commander & Deployed the system, but if truly autonomous, did not control this individual act \\
The manufacturer & Designed hardware; typically shielded by commercial or sovereign immunity \\
The state & Possible under international law, but individual accountability disappears entirely \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{argumentbox}

\parencite{hrw_losing_humanity_2012}
\end{frame}

% ===== IMAGE SLIDE: Campaign to Stop Killer Robots =====
\begin{frame}[shrink=20]{Campaign to Stop Killer Robots}
\begin{columns}
\begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
\begin{block}{The Campaign}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Founded in 2012 following the HRW report \emph{Losing Humanity}
    \item Coalition of NGOs, academics, and former military officials
    \item Calls for a \textbf{preemptive ban} on fully autonomous weapons
    \item Analogous to bans on landmines (Ottawa Treaty, 1997) and cluster munitions (2008)
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\textbf{Counter-argument}: States developing military AI (U.S., China, Russia) argue meaningful human control can coexist with autonomous speed and precision.
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.47\textwidth}
\begin{figure}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth,keepaspectratio]{images/killer_robots_campaign.jpg}
    \caption*{\scriptsize Campaign to Stop Killer Robots meeting, 2013. CC BY 2.0, via Wikimedia Commons.}
\end{figure}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=20]{Case Study: The Kargu-2 in Libya (2020)}
\begin{casestudybox}[The First Autonomous Lethal Attack?]
\small
In 2021, a UN Panel of Experts report described a 2020 incident in Libya:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Government-allied forces deployed \textbf{Kargu-2} loitering munitions (Turkish-made drones with autonomous targeting)
    \item The drones were ``programmed to attack targets without requiring data connectivity \ldots\ no human was in-the-loop''
    \item This may have been the first recorded \textbf{lethal autonomous strike} on human targets
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Ambiguity}: Facts remain disputed; manufacturers denied full autonomy. But the threshold may have quietly already been crossed.
\end{casestudybox}
\begin{discussionbox}[Discussion]
\scriptsize
If fully autonomous weapons are already deployed, does the debate about whether to \emph{allow} them still matter? What regulatory mechanisms remain available?
\end{discussionbox}
\end{frame}

\section{Part IV: AI and Biological Threats}

% ===== IMAGE SLIDE: BSL-4 lab =====
\begin{frame}[shrink=3]{AI in the Lab: Opportunity and Dual-Use Risk}
\begin{columns}
\begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
\textbf{AI has transformed biological research:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AlphaFold} (DeepMind, 2021): predicted the 3D structure of nearly all known proteins, accelerating drug discovery and basic science
    \item AI-assisted genomics: faster pathogen identification and vaccine design
    \item AI-powered drug screening: reduces time and cost of finding therapeutic compounds
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\textbf{But}: the same capabilities that speed up therapeutic research can speed up the design of harmful agents.
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.47\textwidth}
\begin{figure}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth,keepaspectratio]{images/bsl4_researcher.jpg}
    \caption*{\scriptsize BSL-4 researcher in positive-pressure suit. Public domain (NIAID/NIH), via Wikimedia Commons.}
\end{figure}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=10]{The Dual-Use Dilemma}
\begin{conceptbox}[Dual-Use Research of Concern (DURC)]
Research that can be used both to \emph{protect} public health (vaccines, diagnostics, therapeutics) and to \emph{create} biological threats (enhanced pathogens, targeted toxins). The same knowledge is simultaneously beneficial and potentially catastrophic.
\end{conceptbox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\textcite{sandbrink_ai_biological_misuse_2023} draws a critical distinction:

\vspace{0.1cm}
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{4.2cm}p{7.4cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Type of AI} & \textbf{Dual-Use Risk Profile} \\
\midrule
\textbf{LLMs} (GPT, Claude, etc.) & Primarily an \emph{information hazard}: make dangerous knowledge more accessible, but limited novel capability \\
\textbf{Biological design tools} (AlphaFold, RFdiffusion, BindingDB) & More serious: can design \emph{novel} proteins, toxins, or pathogens that do not yet exist \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=7]{Case Study: The Nerve Agent Experiment (2022)}
\begin{casestudybox}[AI Designs VX Analogs in Hours]
\small
In 2022, researchers at Collaborations Pharmaceuticals published a disturbing result:
\begin{itemize}
    \item They \emph{inverted} their drug-discovery AI (designed to avoid toxic compounds) to instead maximize toxicity
    \item In under 6 hours, the model generated approximately \textbf{40,000 candidate molecules}
    \item Many were predicted to be \textbf{more toxic than VX nerve agent}
    \item The researchers halted the experiment and published a warning paper
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Key point}: the barrier to misuse is not primarily computational. It is biological and logistical --- synthesizing and weaponizing such compounds still requires significant expertise and infrastructure.
\end{casestudybox}

\parencite{sandbrink_ai_biological_misuse_2023}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=17]{Governance Responses to AI Biosecurity Risk}
\begin{conceptbox}[Information Hazard]
An ``infohazard'' is information whose spread increases the probability of harm, even if the information itself is true and valuable in other contexts.
\end{conceptbox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
Current and proposed governance tools:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sequence screening}: DNA synthesis companies screen orders for dangerous sequences (select agents, Australia Group pathogen list)
    \item \textbf{Model access controls}: restrict access to high-risk biological design AI; require biosecurity review for fine-tuning on pathogen data
    \item \textbf{Dual-use research oversight}: the U.S.\ NSABB reviews proposals liable to generate dangerous knowledge
    \item \textbf{AI guardrails}: major LLMs are trained to refuse dangerous biological requests --- though jailbreaks and indirect elicitation remain possible
\end{itemize}

\begin{alertblock}{The Core Tension}
\scriptsize
The more powerful and broadly accessible AI tools become, the harder it is to prevent misuse without also suppressing legitimate research and scientific exchange.
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

\section{Part V: Longtermism and the Precautionary Principle}

\begin{frame}[shrink=17]{What Is Longtermism?}
\begin{conceptbox}[Longtermism --- MacAskill (2022)]
``We are living at the most important time in history \ldots\ the decisions we make now could lock in outcomes for billions of years.''

\vspace{0.15cm}
\textbf{Core claims}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Future people matter morally as much as present people
    \item Given low temporal discount rates, the long-run future contains \emph{vastly} more people than the present
    \item Therefore, improving the long-run trajectory of civilization is among our \emph{most} important moral tasks
    \item AI existential risk reduction is accordingly a top moral priority
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{0.1cm}
\scriptsize\textcite{macaskill_future_2022}
\end{conceptbox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
The arithmetic: if humanity lasts one million years at current population, roughly $10^{14}$ future people exist. Even a 0.001\% reduction in extinction probability ``saves'' millions of expected lives.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The Expected Value Argument and Pascal's Mugging}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{argumentbox}[The Expected Value Case for X-Risk]
\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}
    \item Let $P$ = probability of extinction from AI ($\sim$10\% per Ord)
    \item Let $N$ = expected future humans if we survive ($\sim$$10^{14}$)
    \item Expected lives at stake $= P \times N \approx 10^{13}$
    \item No near-term intervention rivals this in expected value
    \item \textbf{Conclusion}: AI safety deserves extraordinary moral priority
\end{itemize}
\end{argumentbox}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{objectionbox}[Pascal's Mugging --- The Skeptical Reply]
\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}
    \item This logic can justify almost anything: multiply a tiny probability by a huge number and nothing else competes
    \item Probabilities of exotic future scenarios are not reliably estimable
    \item Expected value reasoning breaks down under radical uncertainty (``fanaticism'' in decision theory)
    \item We have strong reasons to distrust intuitions about extremely large or small numbers
\end{itemize}
\end{objectionbox}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=5]{The Precautionary Principle}
\begin{conceptbox}[The Precautionary Principle (Wingspread Statement, 1998)]
When an action raises threats of harm to human health or the environment, \textbf{precautionary measures should be taken even if some cause-and-effect relationships are not fully established scientifically}. The burden of proof falls on those who claim the action is safe.
\end{conceptbox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
Applied to AI development:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We do not need to \emph{prove} that superintelligent AI will cause catastrophe
    \item We only need to establish that the risk is non-negligible and the potential harm enormous
    \item \textbf{Therefore}: slow AI development, mandate safety research, impose moratoria on dangerous capabilities
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.2cm}
Precautionary logic underlies the Future of Life Institute's pause letter (2023), many EU AI Act provisions, and calls for a halt to frontier model training.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=14]{Sunstein: The Precautionary Principle Is Incoherent}
\begin{objectionbox}[Sunstein's Critique]
The precautionary principle is paralytic because \textbf{every action \emph{and} inaction carries risks}.
\vspace{0.1cm}

\textbf{Example}: Suppose we impose a moratorium on AI medical research because of dual-use risk:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cancer treatments that AI might have discovered are not developed
    \item Pandemic response tools are delayed
    \item These foregone benefits are \emph{also} harms --- harms of inaction
\end{itemize}
The precautionary principle tells us to be cautious about \emph{both} paths, but cannot tell us \emph{which} path to take. It provides no way to \emph{compare} risks.
\end{objectionbox}

\vspace{0.15cm}
Sunstein's alternative: \textbf{cost-benefit analysis} --- rigorously estimate and compare all costs and benefits of both action and inaction, including the costs of regulation itself.

\parencite{sunstein_laws_of_fear_2005}
\end{frame}

\section{Part VI: Conclusion}

\begin{frame}[allowframebreaks]{Key Concepts}
\footnotesize
\begin{description}
    \item[\textbf{Existential risk}] Any event that permanently annihilates Earth-originating intelligent life or drastically curtails its potential --- the category Bostrom argues advanced AI may soon enter.
    \item[\textbf{Intelligence explosion}] The hypothesis that an AI capable of improving its own design could rapidly bootstrap to vastly superhuman capability through recursive self-improvement.
    \item[\textbf{Orthogonality thesis}] Intelligence and goal-content are independent: a superintelligent system could efficiently pursue \emph{any} objective, including ones catastrophic for humans.
    \item[\textbf{Instrumental convergence}] Whatever its final goal, a capable AI tends toward self-preservation, resource acquisition, and resistance to shutdown --- without being explicitly programmed to.
    \item[\textbf{Alignment problem}] The unsolved challenge of specifying human values precisely enough that a powerful optimizer pursues what we actually want rather than a damaging proxy.
    \item[\textbf{Stochastic parrot}] Bender \& Gebru's term for an LLM that produces statistically plausible text without understanding, semantics, or genuine intentions.
    \item[\textbf{TESCREAL}] Gebru \& Torres's acronym for the ideological bundle (Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, AI safety) whose assumptions are embedded in mainstream x-risk discourse.
    \item[\textbf{Longtermism}] The view that shaping the long-run future is our most important moral task, because future people vastly outnumber people alive today. Championed by MacAskill; challenged by Torres.
    \item[\textbf{Pascal's Mugging}] The objection that expected-value reasoning can justify almost any present sacrifice: multiply a tiny probability by an astronomically large future population and nothing in the present competes.
    \item[\textbf{Precautionary principle}] Take precautionary measures against possible large harms even without full scientific proof, placing the burden on those claiming safety. Applied to AI; critiqued by Sunstein as incoherent.
    \item[\textbf{LAWS}] Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems --- systems designed to select and engage targets without real-time human authorization.
    \item[\textbf{Meaningful human control}] The ethical requirement that a human must be able to authorize each individual use of lethal force. The central contested standard in autonomous weapons governance.
    \item[\textbf{Accountability gap}] The absence of any clear legal or moral responsible party when an autonomous weapon causes unlawful harm --- machine, programmer, commander, manufacturer, and state all evade full responsibility.
    \item[\textbf{Dual-use research of concern (DURC)}] Research that can simultaneously protect public health and enable biological threats; the same tool that designs vaccines can design toxins.
    \item[\textbf{Infohazard}] Information whose spread increases the probability of harm regardless of its truth value --- the key concept for evaluating whether LLMs pose biosecurity risks.
\end{description}

\begin{alertblock}{The Core Tension}
The existential risk debate is partly \emph{empirical} (will AI become catastrophically dangerous?) and partly \emph{distributional} (whose present concerns get displaced when we focus on speculative future harms?).
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Synthesis: Both/And, Not Either/Or}
We do not need to choose between taking existential risk seriously and addressing present-day harms. \textbf{But framing matters}:

\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{``AI will kill everyone''} $\rightarrow$ resources flow to labs and frontier compute; little accountability for present bias and surveillance
    \item \textbf{``AI risk is just hype''} $\rightarrow$ present harms receive attention, but genuine long-term dangers may be neglected until it is too late
    \item \textbf{A better framing}: responsible AI development requires attending to risks at \emph{multiple time horizons}, centering the most affected communities, and maintaining meaningful human control at every level
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{alertblock}{The Political Economy Question}
Who currently sets the agenda on AI risk? What voices are absent from the rooms where these decisions are made?
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Virtue Ethics and the Doomsday Debate}
Vallor's technomoral virtues from Lecture 2 offer a compass for navigating genuine uncertainty:

\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{2.4cm}p{9.2cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Virtue} & \textbf{Applied to AI Risk} \\
\midrule
\textbf{Humility} & Neither dismiss existential risk as science fiction nor claim certainty about timelines we cannot know \\
\addlinespace
\textbf{Justice} & Ensure risk-reduction efforts do not themselves concentrate power or ignore present inequality \\
\addlinespace
\textbf{Courage} & Raise hard questions about capability claims, safety theater, and who benefits from particular framings \\
\addlinespace
\textbf{Care} & Center the needs of those most exposed to AI harms --- in the present and in the future \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink=11]{Final Discussion: The \$1 Billion Question}
\begin{discussionbox}[Resource Allocation Under Uncertainty]
You have \$1\,billion to allocate to reducing AI-related risks. How would you split it between:

\vspace{0.1cm}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(A)] \textbf{AI alignment research}: technical work on ensuring advanced AI systems pursue human-beneficial goals
    \item[(B)] \textbf{Autonomous weapons regulation}: international treaty development, export controls, accountability mechanisms
    \item[(C)] \textbf{Present-day algorithmic bias}: auditing, litigation, regulation, and affected-community organizing around \emph{existing} AI discrimination
    \item[(D)] \textbf{AI biosecurity}: governance of biological design tools, sequence screening, dual-use oversight
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.1cm}
\textbf{Justify your allocation.} What assumptions about probability, severity, and tractability drive your answer?
\end{discussionbox}
\end{frame}
