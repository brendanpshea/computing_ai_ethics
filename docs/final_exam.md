## Overview

The final exam is a **2-hour timed essay exam** taken online with your webcam on. You will see **4 essay prompts** drawn from the **8 possible topics** listed below, and you will choose **2 of the 4** to write. Each essay is graded out of **10 points**, for a total of **20 points**.

The eight possible topics are published in advance so you can prepare. You will not know which four will appear until the exam opens.

## Format and Rules

### Structure
- **2 hours total**, roughly 1 hour per essay (you may allocate your time as you like).
- **2 essays**, each a full **five-paragraph theme** of **5–8 sentences per paragraph**. Short, bulleted, or one-paragraph answers will not receive full credit.

### Allowed Resources
- **One page of handwritten notes**, front and back. Notes must be **handwritten by you** (not typed, not printed, not photocopied).
- At the start of the exam, **hold your notes up to the webcam** — front, then back — so I can see them clearly before you begin writing.
- **No other resources.** No textbooks, slides, websites, classmates, or generative AI (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Copilot, etc.). Use of AI on the final is treated as academic dishonesty per the syllabus.

### What I am grading on
Each essay is worth 10 points, scored on:

| Category | Points |
|---|---|
| **Argument** — clear thesis, logical reasoning, engages a genuine philosophical question | 4 |
| **Course content** — accurate use of concepts, theories, thinkers, and cases from the course | 4 |
| **Writing and structure** — five well-developed paragraphs, clear prose, stays on topic | 2 |

## How to Structure a Five-Paragraph Essay

Every prompt below is an **argumentative, philosophical essay**. You are not just summarizing material — you are taking a position and defending it using course content. A good essay has a clear target you are arguing *for* and a clear target you are arguing *against*.

Use this structure as a default:

1. **Introduction (5–8 sentences).** Open with the problem or case. Briefly explain why it matters. State your **thesis** — the specific claim you will defend — in one clear sentence near the end of the paragraph. Preview the three reasons or moves you will make.

2. **Body Paragraph 1 — First reason or concept.** Introduce a key idea, theory, or thinker from the course (e.g., utilitarianism, virtue ethics, Kant, Searle, Bostrom, Singer). Explain it accurately in your own words. Apply it to the case. End with a sentence connecting this back to your thesis.

3. **Body Paragraph 2 — Second reason, or evidence / example.** Bring in a second course concept or a concrete case (e.g., Clearview AI, the FTX collapse, Getty v. Stability AI, LaMDA, Section 230). Show how it supports your thesis. This is a great place to bring in **your own background, interests, or experience** — a job you've had, a platform you use, a community you belong to — as long as you tie it to the philosophical argument.

4. **Body Paragraph 3 — Objection and response.** State the strongest objection a thoughtful person could raise against your thesis. Take it seriously — steelman it, don't strawman it. Then explain why your position still holds, or concede the objection narrowly while defending your main claim.

5. **Conclusion (5–8 sentences).** Restate your thesis in new words. Summarize how your three paragraphs supported it. End by pointing to a broader implication: a policy, a design choice, a way of living, or an open question that follows from your argument.

### Bringing In Your Own Background

Every prompt below invites you to draw on your own experience, major, job, hobbies, or cultural background. This is a **philosophy class**, not a memoir, so personal material should always be in service of the argument. A good personal example illustrates or tests a philosophical claim — it does not replace one.

## The Eight Possible Topics

### Topic 1 — Games, Play, and a Life Worth Living

Chapter 12 argued that games are not a trivial distraction but a serious subject for philosophy. **Bernard Suits** defined a game as "the voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles" (with a prelusory goal, constitutive rules, and a lusory attitude), and in *The Grasshopper* went further — arguing that in a true utopia, where AI and automation met every practical need, **human life itself would consist of playing games**. **C. Thi Nguyen** argues that games are an art form of agency: designers sculpt the goals, abilities, and constraints the player takes on. Against both, critics worry that video games promote violence, foster addiction ("gaming disorder"), or substitute for a meaningful life rather than enrich one.

Defend a thesis on **one** of the following:
- Is Suits right that a life made up of games could be a genuinely *good* life, or does such a life lack something essential (meaning, contribution, stakes)?
- Do video games, on balance, **build or erode** the virtues discussed in Chapter 2 (self-control, practical wisdom, courage, honesty)?
- As AI eliminates more traditional work, should we embrace games and play as a central source of meaning, or resist that shift?

Your essay should: (1) explain the relevant course concepts accurately — at minimum Suits' definition and one other idea (Nguyen on agency, the magic circle, virtue ethics, Keynes on leisure, or the harm/benefit debate); (2) apply them to a concrete example (a specific game, genre, or case — your own gaming experience is welcome evidence); (3) address the strongest objection to your position. Avoid arguing simply that games are "fun" or "a waste of time" — both are too weak to anchor a philosophical thesis.

### Topic 2 — Attention, Virtue, and the Design of Everyday Tech

Drawing on the **virtue ethics** material from Chapter 2, argue for or against the following claim: *"Platforms like TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat, or YouTube make it structurally harder for users to develop and exercise virtues like self-control, honesty, or practical wisdom."*

Your essay should: (1) explain what virtue ethics means by a "virtue" and why virtues are cultivated through habit; (2) analyze at least one specific design feature (infinite scroll, streaks, algorithmic feeds, notifications, likes) in virtue-ethics terms; (3) consider the counterargument that users are responsible for their own character regardless of design. You may draw on your own use of these platforms, but tie personal examples to philosophical claims.

### Topic 3 — Free Speech, Platforms, and Who Decides

Chapter 3 raised the question of whether private platforms should moderate speech, and who is accountable when they do. Write an argumentative essay defending a clear position on **one** of the following questions:
- Should **Section 230** be reformed, preserved, or repealed?
- Is **deplatforming** ever ethically justified, and if so, under what conditions?
- Do **unpaid moderators** (Reddit mods, Discord admins, Wikipedia editors) function as a legitimate form of self-governance, or as exploited labor that launders corporate responsibility?

Your essay should: (1) define the key terms and explain the stakes; (2) defend your position using at least one ethical concept from the course (e.g., Mill on free speech, harm principle, rights vs. utility); (3) address the strongest objection from the opposing side. Personal experience as a moderator, poster, or community member is welcome as illustration.

### Topic 4 — Who Owns What AI Makes?

Chapter 4 examined intellectual property, and Chapters 7–8 examined how generative AI is trained on human-made content. Argue for a clear position on the following: *"When a generative AI model produces an image, song, or piece of writing, the moral rights to that output should belong to [the user / the model developer / the artists whose work trained it / no one — it should be public domain]."*

Your essay should: (1) explain the traditional philosophical justifications for intellectual property (Lockean labor, utilitarian incentive, personality-based views); (2) apply your chosen justification to the AI case and defend your answer; (3) address at least one rival position fairly. Cases like **Getty v. Stability AI**, **GitHub Copilot**, or the **2023 writers' strike** are natural anchors. If you are an artist, writer, coder, or musician, bring that perspective in.

### Topic 5 — Surveillance and the Right to Privacy

Chapter 6 introduced multiple frames for thinking about surveillance: government vs. corporate, consent-based vs. coerced, chilling effects on behavior, and the contextual integrity of information. Defend a thesis on the following: *"There is a meaningful right to privacy in digital life, and [X form of surveillance] violates it in a way that [Y form] does not."*

You pick X and Y. Options include Ring/Neighbors, Clearview AI, period-tracking apps post-Dobbs, school monitoring tools (Gaggle, GoGuardian), China's Social Credit System, or ad-tracking. Your essay should: (1) articulate what grounds the right to privacy (autonomy, dignity, democratic participation, etc.); (2) show why your chosen case X violates that ground; (3) explain why Y does not, or does so less severely. Be specific — "it's creepy" is not an argument.

### Topic 6 — Does AI Actually Understand Anything?

Chapter 7 contrasted two views of AI cognition: **Searle's Chinese Room argument**, which holds that symbol manipulation is not genuine understanding, and the view that sufficiently complex information processing *is* what understanding consists of (sometimes called functionalism). Argue for one of these views as it applies to **modern large language models** (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini).

Your essay should: (1) explain the Chinese Room thought experiment and what Searle was trying to prove; (2) describe, at a conceptual level, how LLMs actually work (training on text, next-token prediction, no grounded experience); (3) defend your position and address the strongest counterargument. If you use AI tools regularly for school, work, or creative projects, your observations are fair evidence — but the argument must be philosophical, not just anecdotal.

### Topic 7 — AI, Work, and What Jobs Are For

Chapter 8 examined AI's impact on writers, artists, programmers, radiologists, and other knowledge workers. Philosophers disagree about what work is *for*: a means to income, a source of meaning and identity, a contribution to the common good, or something we should want less of. Defend a thesis about how society **should** respond to AI-driven displacement in a specific field of your choice.

Your essay should: (1) describe the displacement happening in your chosen field, using course material; (2) articulate a philosophical account of why that kind of work matters (or doesn't); (3) defend a specific response — retraining, UBI, regulation, slowing deployment, embracing it — and address the main objection. Your own career plans, current job, or family's work history are very welcome here, as long as they're tied to the philosophical argument.

### Topic 8 — Do AI Systems Have (or Will They Have) Moral Status?

Chapter 11 asked whether AI systems could ever be proper objects of moral concern — beings we could wrong, harm, or owe something to. Thinkers we discussed offer different criteria: **sentience** (Singer), **rationality and autonomy** (Kant), **relational or social standing**, or **behavioral sophistication**. Argue for a clear position on the following: *"Current or near-future AI systems [do / do not] have morally significant interests that we are obligated to consider."*

Your essay should: (1) pick and defend a criterion for moral status from the course; (2) apply it to a specific case (LaMDA and Blake Lemoine, Replika companions, Character.AI, Sophia the Robot, or the Character.AI lawsuit); (3) address the strongest objection from a rival criterion. Be careful not to slide between "AI *seems* conscious" and "AI *is* conscious" — that distinction is often where essays on this topic succeed or fail.

## Studying Tips

- **Don't memorize essays.** Graders can tell, and pre-written essays rarely match the specific prompt. Memorize **concepts, thinkers, and cases** instead.
- **Build your note sheet around the eight topics**, not the whole course. One corner per topic is a reasonable layout.
- **Practice writing one full essay under time** before the exam. Most students underestimate how fast an hour passes.
- **Know 2–3 concrete cases cold** (names, dates, what happened, why it matters). A specific, accurate case beats three vague gestures at "things we've seen."
- **Every essay needs a thesis.** If you can't state yours in one sentence, you don't have one yet.
